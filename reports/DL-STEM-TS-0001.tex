\hypertarget{introduction}{%
\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}}

This document constitutes the Test Specification (D5) of activity WP2300 within work package WP2000 of the DL-STEM project. 
Its primary objective is to define rigorous evaluation metrics and testing methodologies for the algorithms and datasets specified 
in D2 and D3, targeting the three operational use cases introduced in D4.

\subsection{Document Structure}

This document is organised as follows. Section~\ref{Scope of the Test} defines 
the scope of testing, covering the key evaluation dimensions. 
Section~\ref{Test dataset} describes the test datasets selected for quantitative 
evaluation, including an overview of the candidate datasets considered and their 
train/validation/test splits. Section~\ref{Evaluation Metrics and Performance Assessment} 
specifies the evaluation metrics and performance assessment methodology for both 
the SOT and MOT tasks, and provides reference performance ranges drawn from 
published baselines for contextual comparison.

\hypertarget{Scope of the Test}{%
\section{Scope of the Test}\label{Scope of the Test}}

In accordance with the requirements set out in ESA-EOP-SG-SOW-0563 Appendix~A.5, 
model performance on the evaluation datasets is assessed in terms of both general 
performance and performance across the following test dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Object size} --- Performance is reported separately for targets of varying sizes, distinguishing between small and large objects to reflect the particular challenges posed by sub-pixel and near-pixel targets characteristic of satellite video.

    \item \textbf{Frame rate (FPS)} --- Performance is evaluated across datasets with varying frame rates, as lower frame rates introduce significant challenges for inter-frame target association and motion estimation, particularly when targets lack distinguishing visual features.

    \item \textbf{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)} --- SNR is considered as a key evaluation dimension, with particular emphasis on infrared datasets where weak target signatures and sensor noise present additional detection challenges.

    \item \textbf{Motion blur and occlusion} --- Rather than treating these as dataset-level attributes, performance under motion blur and occlusion is reported at the frame level, isolating and evaluating specifically those frames within each video sequence in which these degradations occur.
\end{enumerate}

\hypertarget{Evaluation Metrics and Performance Assessment}{%
\section{Evaluation Metrics and Performance Assessment}\label{Evaluation Metrics and Performance Assessment}}

To ensure a comprehensive and objective evaluation of the proposed methods, different quantitative metrics are defined according to the nature of each task. 
The selected metrics are widely adopted in the object detection and tracking communities and allow fair comparison across models and experimental settings. 

\hypertarget{Evaluation Metrics - SOT}{%
\subsection{Evaluation Metrics -- SOT}\label{Evaluation Metrics - SOT}}

Single Object Tracking (SOT) is the task of continuously localising a single target object across all frames of a video sequence, given only its bounding box in the first frame as a reference. Unlike detection, 
the model receives no class label at test time and must propagate spatial information over time in the presence of motion, occlusion, and appearance change. Three standard OTB-style metrics are adopted to assess tracking performance.

\subsubsection*{Success Plot and AUC}

The \textbf{success plot} measures the fraction of frames in which the Intersection over Union (IoU) between the predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes exceeds a given threshold $\tau \in [0, 1]$. The \textbf{Success AUC} is the area under this curve, summarising overlap accuracy into a single scalar. A higher AUC indicates that the tracker maintains tighter bounding-box alignment throughout the sequence.

\subsubsection*{Precision Plot and P@20}

The \textbf{precision plot} measures the fraction of frames in which the Euclidean distance between the centre of the predicted box and the centre of the ground-truth box is below a pixel threshold $d \in [0, 50]$. The standard scalar summary \textbf{P@20} is the precision value at $d = 20$ pixels, which represents a commonly used tolerance for small target localisation. A higher P@20 indicates more accurate centre localisation across the sequence.

\subsubsection*{Mean IoU}

\textbf{Mean IoU} is the average overlap between predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes across all frames, providing a direct measure of spatial alignment quality independent of a threshold. A higher mean IoU reflects tighter and more consistent bounding-box predictions throughout the sequence.

\hypertarget{Evaluation Metrics - MOT}{%
\subsection{Evaluation Metrics -- MOT}\label{Evaluation Metrics - MOT}}

Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) is the task of simultaneously detecting and maintaining consistent identities for multiple targets across all frames of a video, without any initialisation provided by the user. At test time, the model receives each frame as input and must jointly perform detection and association: it detects all relevant objects in the current frame and links each detection to an existing track or opens a new one. The final output is a set of trajectories, each assigned a unique and persistent identity throughout the sequence.

%In this work, MOT is evaluated on the BIRDSAI\_MOT dataset, which contains two object categories: \texttt{animal} (class~0) and \texttt{human} (class~1). 
%The model is applied to each test video independently and produces per-frame bounding boxes with associated track IDs, which are matched against ground-truth annotations using the standard MOT evaluation protocol.

The evaluation metrics are grouped into two categories: those that measure \emph{detection quality} per frame, and those that measure \emph{tracking quality} over time.

\subsubsection*{Detection Metrics}

Detection metrics evaluate how accurately the model localises objects in each frame, independently of identity consistency across frames.

\textbf{Precision and Recall.} Precision is the fraction of predicted bounding boxes that correctly match a ground-truth object (IoU\,$\geq$\,0.5), and Recall is the fraction of ground-truth objects that are successfully detected:
\[
  \text{Precision} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}}, \qquad
  \text{Recall} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FN}}
\]
where TP, FP, and FN denote true positives, false positives, and false negatives respectively. Both are higher-is-better; low recall indicates missed targets while low precision indicates excessive false alarms.

\textbf{AP50.} The COCO-style Average Precision at IoU threshold 0.5, averaged over confidence thresholds. It summarises the precision--recall trade-off of the detector into a single scalar. Higher is better.

\textbf{AR\textsubscript{100}.} The Average Recall when at most 100 detections per image are considered. It measures the upper bound of recall achievable by the detector under a fixed detection budget. Higher is better.

\subsubsection*{Tracking Metrics}

Tracking metrics evaluate the consistency of target identities across frames, going beyond per-frame detection accuracy.

\textbf{MOTA.} The Multi-Object Tracking Accuracy jointly penalises false positives, false negatives, and identity switches relative to the total number of ground-truth objects:
\[
  \text{MOTA} = 1 - \frac{\text{FP} + \text{FN} + \text{IDSW}}{\text{GT}}
\]
MOTA ranges from $-\infty$ to 1; higher is better. A negative value indicates that the total number of errors exceeds the number of ground-truth objects. Because MOTA incorporates both detection and association errors, it serves as a holistic indicator of overall tracking system quality.

\textbf{IDF1.} The Identity F1 score is computed over identity-matched detections. For each ground-truth identity, the tracker prediction with the greatest temporal overlap is selected as its match:
\[
  \text{IDF1} = \frac{2\,\text{IDTP}}{2\,\text{IDTP} + \text{IDFP} + \text{IDFN}}
\]
where IDTP, IDFP, and IDFN are the counts of correctly identity-matched detections, detections assigned to a wrong identity, and missed identity-matched detections respectively. IDF1 specifically rewards long, unbroken tracks with consistent identities. Higher is better.

\textbf{ID Switches.} The total number of times a tracked target is assigned a new identity compared to its previous correct assignment. Unlike MOTA and IDF1, this is a raw count where \emph{fewer is better}, and it directly reflects the stability of the association step.
