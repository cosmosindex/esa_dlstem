\begin{abstract}
Remote sensing with spaceborne videos is a rapidly emerging field, yet it is
currently fragmented by disparate datasets that vary in quality, annotation
style, and sensory focus. We introduce Space-tracker, a unified, large-scale,
and multi-sensory benchmark dataset created through the strategic merging and
curation of nine existing spaceborne and aerial datasets, such as SatSOT,
SV248S, and OOTB. Space-tracker addresses the ``paradigm gap'' in remote sensing
by providing a single point of access to multi-task annotations for object
detection, single-object tracking, and multi-object tracking across multimodal
video sequences, with spectrum ranging from visible/near-infrared to
thermal-infrared. Our main contribution lies in the unification of labels and
attributes, where we have harmonized diverse formats into a consistent
COCO-style framework and applied 18 fine-grained attributes to classify
challenges rarely seen in natural-video benchmarks, such as tiny targets
($\sqrt{w \cdot h} < 8$ px, often featureless and near the sensor noise floor),
non-stationary backgrounds induced by platform ego-motion, long-duration
occlusion, and severe motion blur from low-frame-rate high-velocity acquisition.
By benchmarking seven state-of-the-art single-object trackers and seven
state-of-the-art multi-object trackers, including large Foundation Models, we
provide the most comprehensive evaluation to date on the tracking of
featureless, sub-pixel targets from space.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\noindent\textbf{Motivation.}
The rapid advancement of space technology has brought us to a new era of Earth
observation, transitioning from static imagery to real-time dynamic monitoring.
The emergence of high-resolution video satellite constellations—such as Jilin-1
and SkySat has made it possible to gaze continuously at specific ground areas
with high temporal resolution. This shift from static snapshots to continuous
video streams provides a wealth of spatiotemporal information that was
previously inaccessible, representing one of the current frontiers in remote
sensing.

The motivation for Space-tracker stems from the extreme scarcity and
fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne video data. While numerous specialized
datasets have been released—some focusing on specific platforms (e.g., Jilin-1),
others on single categories (e.g., ships), and some only on thermal
imagery—there is no unified standard that allows researchers to develop and
evaluate universal trackers. First, the "small object" problem remains the
primary bottleneck; targets in satellite videos are often reduced to
near-featureless bright spots, making texture-based discrimination nearly
impossible. Second, existing datasets use inconsistent annotation formats (HBB,
OBB, or polygons) and diverse attribute definitions, which hinders the training
of robust, generalizable models like Transformers. Third, there is a lack of
multi-sensory consistency; many tracking pipelines assume perfect RGB
conditions, yet real-world spaceborne applications require the ability to switch
between visible and thermal modalities seamlessly. Space-tracker is motivated by
the need to standardize these diverse streams into a single, high-precision
benchmark that enables the community to tackle the unique geometric and
radiometric challenges of the spaceborne domain under a unified protocol.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution of this paper.}
The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\noindent\textbf{Construction of Space-tracker, a unified benchmark dataset.} We
introduce the largest and most diverse curated benchmark to date for spaceborne
video analytics. By strategically merging and cleaning eight disparate
datasets—including SatSOT, VISO, SV248S, OOTB, and BIRDSAI—we provide a single
point of access to over 1 million annotated instances. This unified repository
addresses the extreme scarcity and fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne
data, providing valuable resource for training robust geospatial models.

\noindent\textbf{Harmonized Multi-Task and Multi-Sensory Annotations.} Unlike previous
benchmarks limited to single tasks or sensors, Space-tracker supports a
multi-task framework encompassing object detection, single-object tracking
(SOT), and multi-object tracking (MOT). We have harmonized all labels into a
consistent COCO-style format and unified the attribute tagging system across the
merged datasets. Furthermore, we integrate multimodal sequences (VNIR and TIR),
enabling researchers to develop trackers that generalize across visible and
thermal spectra for all-weather monitoring

\noindent\textbf{Comprehensive Benchmarking of seven SOTA Models.} We provide an
extensive evaluation of the current state of the art, benchmarking XX backbone
models and XX trackers across four major paradigms: traditional Discriminative
Correlation Filters (DCF), deep Siamese networks, modern spatiotemporal
Transformers, and Geospatial Foundation Models like SAM2. Our analysis offers
new insights into how these architectures handle small objects and long-term
occlusions in non-stationary backgrounds.


\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Generic Visual Object Tracking.}

Siamese-based trackers reformulate tracking as a similarity learning problem.
\citet{bertinetto2016siamfc} introduce a fully convolutional Siamese framework
for real-time tracking, while subsequent works improve localization accuracy
through region proposal networks and deeper backbones \citep{li2018siamrpn,
li2019siamrpn++}. In particular, SiamRPN++~\citep{li2019siamrpn++} remains a
representative Siamese tracker and serves as a strong classical baseline in our
experiments.

More recently, Transformer-based trackers have become dominant by modeling
global context and long-range dependencies. STARK~\citep{yan2021stark}
formulates tracking as direct box prediction, while OSTrack~\citep{ye2022joint}
unifies feature extraction and relation modeling in a one-stream Transformer
architecture. Beyond conventional template-matching paradigms, recent trackers
have further explored object discovery and large-scale tracking architectures.
ODTrack~\citep{zheng2024odtrack} introduces object-discovery cues for robust
tracking, and LoRAT-g378~\citep{lin2024tracking} represents a recent large-scale
tracker with strong generalization capability. These trackers achieve strong
performance on natural video benchmarks, but they are primarily designed for
scenes with targets that are relatively large, visually distinctive, and
well-textured. Their robustness under spaceborne conditions, especially for
objects smaller than a few dozen pixels, remains underexplored.

Recent advances in vision foundation models further reshape the tracking
landscape. SAM~2~\citep{ravi2024sam} extends promptable segmentation to images
and videos, while SAMURAI~\citep{yang2026samurai} adapts SAM-style
representations for robust visual tracking. More recently,
SAM~3~\citep{carion2026sam3} further advances foundation-model-based object
understanding. These models demonstrate strong generalization across
natural-image and natural-video domains, but are primarily trained on objects
with clear boundaries and sufficient spatial extent. In spaceborne videos,
targets are often extremely small, low-contrast, and sparsely textured, making
promptable segmentation and foundation-model-based tracking substantially more
challenging. Therefore, evaluating these models on our benchmark provides a
direct test of whether modern foundation models can generalize to the extremely
small-object regime.

\noindent\textbf{Satellite Video Object Tracking.} Satellite video tracking
poses unique challenges, including extremely small object sizes, often below
$30$ pixels, low contrast, weak appearance cues, complex platform motion, and
background clutter. Early satellite tracking approaches extend correlation
filters with motion modeling to improve robustness in satellite videos
\citep{lin2024motion}. Siamese-based methods have also been adapted to this
domain by incorporating high-resolution representations and motion fitting
strategies \citep{ruan2022deep, shao2021hrsiam}. More recently,
Transformer-based approaches have been explored to capture spatio-temporal
dependencies in satellite imagery \citep{lai2023target}.

Despite these advances, existing satellite-tracking studies are often evaluated
against limited benchmarks and do not fully meet the requirements of modern
spaceborne video understanding. In particular, existing benchmarks remain
insufficient in three aspects: they often focus on either detection or tracking
rather than a unified detection-and-tracking setting; many annotations rely on
axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack oriented bounding boxes (OBBs), which are
critical for objects with arbitrary orientations in aerial views; and their
scenario diversity, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions
remain limited. These limitations make it difficult to systematically evaluate
both classical trackers and recent foundation models in the extremely
small-object regime.

\noindent\textbf{Benchmarks for Satellite Detection and Tracking.} Existing
tracking benchmarks such as OTB~\citep{wu2013otb} and
LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}mainly focus on natural videos and do not capture the
unique challenges of satellite imagery. In the satellite domain,
SatSOT~\citep{satSOT2022} introduces a densely annotated benchmark for satellite
video single-object tracking, and SV248S~\citep{Li2022SV248S} further expands
the scale of satellite SOT evaluation. OOTB~\citep{CHEN2024212ootb} provides an
oriented tracking benchmark and highlights the importance of oriented bounding
boxes (OBBs) for aerial object localization.

Despite these efforts, existing datasets exhibit several key limitations: (1)
extremely small object regime: most benchmarks do not explicitly small target
objects, which are common in spaceborne videos; (2) task fragmentation: datasets
are typically designed for either detection or tracking, lacking a unified
benchmark for joint evaluation; (3) lack of oriented annotations: most datasets
rely on axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack-oriented bounding boxes (OBBs),
which are critical for accurately localizing objects with arbitrary orientations
in aerial views; and (4) limited diversity: existing datasets cover a limited
range of scenarios, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions,
restricting their ability to evaluate model generalization.



\section{Our Dataset}
\noindent\textbf{Design principle.}
Space-tracker is constructed under the principle of multi-sensory and
multi-platform diversity. To overcome the limitation of single-source
benchmarks, we integrate XX heterogeneous datasets, including SatSOT, VISO,
SV248S, OOTB, and BIRDSAI, into a unified benchmark for aerospace analytics.
The dataset covers imagery from multiple platforms and constellations,
including Jilin-1 sub-meter RGB, SkySat, and the International Space Station
(ISS), and spans both visible--near-infrared (VNIR) and thermal infrared
(TIR) modalities.

Space-tracker focuses on tracking small objects in complex aerospace scenes.
We prioritize sequences in which targets occupy fewer than 10 pixels, where
limited spatial evidence, sub-pixel motion, and weak appearance cues pose
substantial challenges to tracking. The selected sequences also contain
complex background structures over multiple spatial scales. These backgrounds
may evolve at rates similar to the targets, introduce distractors, or cause
frequent occlusions. The dataset further covers diverse geographic regions,
including China, the United States, Libya, and Turkey, and varied temporal
conditions, with frame rates ranging from 3 to 30 FPS.

\noindent\textbf{Pre-processing workflow.}
We convert fragmented sources into a consistent benchmark using a unified
preprocessing pipeline. First, annotations from XML, JSON, and
coordinate-only files are aggregated and converted into a COCO-style
classification and detection format. Bounding boxes are aligned to a shared
coordinate convention, and class identifiers are unified across datasets to
support consistent training and evaluation.

We further harmonize source-specific difficulty labels into a unified set of
18 fine-grained attributes tailored to spaceborne video analytics. For
example, original labels such as partial occlusion (PO) and full occlusion
(FO) are mapped into a common convention, enabling trackers to be evaluated
under consistent definitions of difficulty. Finally, we manually inspect and
refine the annotations using an internally developed tool. This step
corrects misaligned targets and adds labels for previously unannotated large
objects, such as cattle and specific vehicles, ensuring high-quality ground
truth across all merged sources.

\noindent\textbf{Object size regime definition.} In addition to the attributes introduced
above, we further define the notions of \emph{small object} and \emph{tiny
object} based on the empirical behaviour of all evaluated trackers. A
distinctive characteristic of remote-sensing datasets is that objects which
appear large in the physical world can become vanishingly small on the image
plane: a car travelling at high speed may occupy no more than a faint point of
light in a satellite video frame. Regardless of the object's real-world
identity, the only information available to a deep-learning tracker is the
visual evidence contained within the annotated bounding box, so its pixel
size---rather than the object's physical extent---is what determines tracking
difficulty. A bounding box of size $s$ pixels presents the same visual evidence
to a tracker regardless of the sensor's GSD, which motivates a unified,
pixel-based criterion applicable across datasets with heterogeneous GSDs.

We partition every frame by the ground-truth bounding-box scale
$s=\sqrt{\mathrm{area}}$ and examine how tracker performance varies with $s$.
Figure~\ref{fig:sr_npr_vs_bbox_size} plots the mean Success AUC (SR) and
Normalised Precision AUC (NPR) of the seven evaluated trackers against $s$ on
OOTB, SatSOT, and SV248S, with $2$-pixel bins over $s\in[0,32]\,$px. Across all
three datasets the curves share a consistent shape: trackers perform poorly
($\mathrm{SR}<0.3$) for $s<8\,$px, and recover to a moderate regime
($\mathrm{SR}\approx 0.3\text{--}0.5$) between $8$ and $16\,$px. Guided by these
common inflection points, we adopt a unified, dataset-agnostic
partition---\emph{tiny} ($s<8\,$px) and \emph{small} ($8\le s<16\,$px)---which
we use throughout the remainder of the paper. Thresholds are kept identical
across datasets so that size-stratified results are directly comparable; the
apparent sensitivity of SV248S at larger scales reflects sparse bin support
($<200$ frames per bin beyond $18\,$px) rather than a genuinely different
size--difficulty relationship.

\noindent\textbf{Dataset Overview.}
\label{sec:dataset_overview}

Space-Tracker-SOT consolidates the three publicly available SOT benchmarks
for spaceborne video, namely SatSOT~\citep{satSOT2022},
SV248S~\citep{Li2022SV248S}, and OOTB~\citep{CHEN2024212ootb}, into a single
unified benchmark of 463 sequences and 214{,}175 frames acquired by the
Jilin-1, SkySat, and ISS platforms (Table~\ref{tab:spaceborne_video_datasets}).
The three source datasets complement each other in scale, sensor, and
labeling style. SatSOT and SV248S are acquired exclusively by Jilin-1 and
annotated with axis-aligned boxes, with SV248S additionally providing tight
polygon masks. OOTB spans three platforms and uses oriented bounding boxes.
Taken together they cover the four object categories that dominate practical
spaceborne tracking, namely car, ship, plane, and train, across diverse
urban, coastal, and airport scenes.

\noindent\textbf{Annotation density and absent-target frames.} For each SOT
sequence the tracked target is single by construction, so the number of
bounding-box labels is at most the number of frames. The gap between
\#Frames and \#Labels in Table~\ref{tab:spaceborne_video_datasets} reflects
frames in which the target is absent or fully occluded. SatSOT marks such
frames as \texttt{none}, SV248S flags them with an invisibility state, and
OOTB labels every frame so its \#Labels equals \#Frames. We preserve these
conventions during integration rather than imputing missing boxes, so that
long-term occlusion and out-of-view behavior remain faithfully recoverable
downstream.

\noindent\textbf{Object scale.} A defining characteristic of spaceborne SOT
is the prevalence of extremely small targets. We define an annotation as a
\emph{tiny object} when the square root of its bounding-box area falls below
$8$ pixels, equivalently when the area is below $64$\,px$^2$. Under this
criterion, $63.1\%$ of all annotations in Space-Tracker-SOT are tiny, and
SV248S alone contributes an $80.3\%$ tiny ratio. This is a regime in which
appearance, texture, and shape cues that anchor most modern trackers
effectively vanish, a point we return to in the attribute discussion below.

\noindent\textbf{Frame rate.} Native acquisition rates range from 10 to
30~fps across the source platforms. One subtlety is worth flagging: SV248S
is distributed at 25~fps after video-frame-interpolation re-encoding from a
native 10~fps signal, so its temporal statistics should be interpreted
accordingly. We retain the as-distributed frame rate for evaluation to
ensure reproducibility against prior work.

\noindent\textbf{Toward a unified attribute taxonomy.} Each source benchmark
ships its own sequence-level attribute set, with 11 in SatSOT, 10 in SV248S,
and 12 in OOTB, and the definitions overlap but are not consistent (what
counts as ``occlusion'' or ``background clutter'' varies across the three).
To support consistent evaluation across the merged benchmark, we align these
into a single taxonomy of 18 fine-grained attributes, retaining the
discriminative power of each source while eliminating definitional
conflicts. The resulting attribute set is more than a relabeling. As we show
next, several of these attributes describe failure modes whose severity in
spaceborne video is qualitatively different from, and in many cases not
reducible to, their natural-video namesakes.

\noindent\textbf{Attribute taxonomy.} Following the conventions established
in SatSOT~\citep{satSOT2022}, SV248S~\citep{Li2022SV248S}, and
OOTB~\citep{chen2024ootb}, we consolidate 18 fine-grained attributes that
together characterize the challenges of single object tracking in spaceborne
video. Many of these attributes share names with those in natural-video
benchmarks such as OTB~\citep{wu2015otb}, LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, and
TrackingNet~\citep{muller2018trackingnet}, but the underlying phenomena
differ substantially in both nature and severity. Below we discuss several
attributes whose meanings have been quietly redefined by the satellite
domain, and which expose failure modes that the broader tracking community
has rarely examined.

\noindent\textbf{Tiny Object (TO) is not a long-tail case but the default
regime.} In OTB and UAV123, an object is considered ``low resolution'' below
400 and 1000 pixels respectively, and in LaSOT and TrackingNet the threshold
is 1000 pixels, all measured by bounding-box area. In our benchmark we adopt
a definition better aligned with the spatial scale of satellite imagery: an
object is considered tiny when the square root of its bounding-box area is
below 8 pixels ($\sqrt{\text{area}} < 8$). Under this criterion, a typical
car occupies only a few dozen pixels in total area and roughly 4 to 8 pixels
along its diagonal, which is two to three orders of magnitude fewer usable
pixels than under the LaSOT threshold. At this scale the object reduces to
a handful of intensity values, and the shape, texture, and color cues that
anchor most modern Siamese and Transformer trackers effectively vanish.
Generic trackers benchmarked on natural video have rarely been stress-tested
in this regime, and as our experiments show
(Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), even SOTA trackers degrade sharply once
appearance features are no longer discriminative.

\noindent\textbf{Similar Appearance (SA) and Dense Similarity (DS) operate
under feature scarcity.} Natural-video benchmarks include similar-object
distractors, for example a tracked person in a crowd, but the distractors
and the target still carry rich appearance cues that allow a learned
embedding to separate them. In SV, a tracked vehicle is often visually
indistinguishable from neighboring vehicles, lane markings, manhole covers,
or even bright pixels on a zebra crossing, because \emph{all} such entities
collapse to a few-pixel bright blob under the satellite GSD. SV248S
formalizes this with its Background Cluster (BCL) attribute, defined as the
target merging into its background for at least 10 consecutive frames. This
phenomenon has no clean counterpart in OTB or LaSOT, where ``background
clutter'' still assumes the object remains visually distinct. The CV
community has generally treated distractor-aware tracking as an
embedding-quality problem; under SV conditions it becomes an
\emph{information-availability} problem, since the discriminative signal may
simply not exist in the pixel data.

\noindent\textbf{Isotropic Motion (IM) and Anisotropic Motion (AM) introduce
a motion-cue ambiguity absent from natural-video tracking.} Vehicles on the
same road segment travel with near-identical speed and direction, so motion
features, which are often used as a fallback when appearance fails, also
collapse: surrounding cars in IM scenarios provide nearly the same
optical-flow signature as the target. AM is the inverse failure mode, where
oncoming traffic produces strong but oppositely-signed flow that confuses
motion-based association. OOTB introduced these attributes precisely because
the standard appearance-plus-motion complementarity assumed in natural-video
tracking does not hold under satellite imaging geometry.

\noindent\textbf{Long-Term Occlusion (LTO) is structural, not incidental.}
In OTB and LaSOT, occlusion is typically caused by transient obstacles such
as a passing pedestrian or a hand. In SV, occlusion is dominated by the
urban built environment, including overpasses, building shadows, and dense
tree canopies, and SV248S sets the LTO threshold at 50 consecutive occluded
frames. The bird's-eye view means that re-identification after occlusion
cannot rely on the rich appearance memory that natural-video re-detection
trackers exploit. The tracker has only motion priors and a few-pixel
template to work with.

\noindent\textbf{Out-of-Normal (OON) aspect ratio reflects a category bias
absent from natural video.} Trains in SV routinely exhibit aspect ratios
above 10:1, far outside the $[0.3, 3]$ range typical of natural-video
objects. Trackers trained on LaSOT or GOT-10k have effectively never seen
such aspect ratios at training time, and HBB-based outputs become severely
misaligned with object extent, a problem invisible in benchmarks that
assume roughly square or moderately elongated objects.

\noindent\textbf{Natural Disturbance (ND) and Background Jitter (BJT) are
imaging-geometry artifacts.} Cloud cover, atmospheric haze, and
platform-induced inter-frame jitter are characteristic of spaceborne
sensing and have no analog in handheld or UAV-mounted cameras. They affect
entire frames globally rather than locally, violating the locality
assumption built into most attention-based tracking heads.

\noindent\textbf{Summary.} Taken together these attributes show that SV
tracking is not just a harder version of natural-video tracking but a
regime with qualitatively different failure modes. The community has built
strong solutions for distractors, occlusion, and scale variation under the
assumption that the target remains visually informative. SV systematically
violates that assumption, and the 18 attributes in our benchmark are
designed to expose exactly this gap.

\section{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
\noindent\textbf{Model selection.}
\label{sec:model_selection}
To establish a robust performance baseline for Space-tracker, we adopt a
selection logic that includes diverse methodology lines. Our goal is to evaluate
how different architectural evolutions, ranging from classical supervised
pipelines to modern self-supervised foundation models, handle the unique
constraints of tiny and even featureless targets in spaceborne video. We
categorize our benchmarked methods into three primary methodological
trajectories:

\noindent\textbf{Region Proposal and Detection-Based Tracking.} We select representative
models from the early deep learning era that established the foundation for
supervised tracking. This includes two-stage baselines like Faster R-CNN and
paradigms like Tracktor, which leverage region proposal networks (RPN) to
identify targets and subsequently associate them across frames. These models
serve as a classical reference for understanding the performance of explicit,
frame-by-frame supervised detection in satellite imagery.

\noindent\textbf{Spatiotemporal Fusion Models.} To address the limitations of independent
frame processing, we select architectures that explicitly model the time
dimension. This line includes spatiotemporal Transformers (e.g., HRLT, TATrans)
and joint detection and tracking (JDT) frameworks that couple appearance and
motion features during training. We also include unified fusion frameworks like
STAR, which integrates deep temporal features and physical motion priors (e.g.,
Kalman filters) to maintain identity consistency during long-term occlusions in
non-stationary backgrounds.

\noindent\textbf{Large Geospatial and Vision Foundation Models.} Reflecting the most
recent paradigm shift, we evaluate large-scale foundation models such as SAM 2
and DINO v3. These models are selected for their robust zero-shot generalization
and ability to leverage high-quality feature representations learned from vast
amounts of unlabeled data. By evaluating prompt-driven video segmentation and
tracking, we explore the potential of transfer learning to overcome the extreme
scarcity of labeled spaceborne data.

\noindent\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}

\noindent\textbf{Single Object Tracking.}
We evaluate SOT trackers using four complementary metrics---Success Rate
(SR), Normalized Precision (NPR), Precision (PR), and P@5---chosen to align
with the dominant convention in modern generic-vision SOT
benchmarks~\citep{muller2018trackingnet,fan2019lasot,huang2021got10k} while
preserving the small-object diagnostic granularity required by satellite
video. Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.1. For overall
performance we report SR, NPR, and PR, capturing IoU-based overlap,
scale-normalized localization, and
pixel-level center accuracy respectively. For the \emph{tiny-object subset}---%
sequences with median GT side length $\sqrt{w_t^{\mathrm{gt}} h_t^{\mathrm{gt}}} < 8$~px
(the satellite analogue of COCO's $32$~px small-object
cutoff~\citep{lin2014coco})---we replace PR with P@5, since PR's $[0,50]$~px
integration range loses discriminative meaning once typical errors exceed the
target's full extent. Higher is better for all four metrics.

\noindent\textbf{Object Detection.} We evaluate detectors using mean Average
Precision at IoU threshold $0.5$ ($\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$) as our primary ranking
metric, complemented by class-agnostic $\mathrm{AP}_{\text{overall}}$ and
operating-point Precision (P), Recall (R), and F1 at a fixed score threshold
of $0.5$. We deliberately adopt single-threshold $\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$ rather than 
the COCO-style $\mathrm{mAP}_{[.5:.95]}$ aggregate~\citep{lin2014coco}, since targets in
spaceborne video are typically smaller than $20\times 20$ pixels, where
strict IoU thresholds ($\geq 0.75$) tend to reflect sub-pixel localization
noise rather than genuine detector capability; single-threshold
$\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$ is also the prevailing convention in spaceborne
small-object detection~\citep{yin2022satmtb,he2022airmot}.
$\mathrm{AP}_{\text{overall}}$ merges predictions from all classes into a
single foreground category before constructing the PR curve, isolating
localization quality from the long-tailed class distribution typical of
satellite video. P, R, and F1 at a fixed threshold reveal
deployment-relevant behavior, such as over-confident false positives in
cluttered backgrounds and missed dim or sub-pixel targets, which
threshold-averaged AP tends to obscure. Higher is better for all five
metrics.
% Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.2.

\noindent\textbf{Multi-Object Tracking.} We evaluate MOT performance using a
combination of HOTA-family, CLEAR-family, and Identity-family metrics
computed under the standard MOTChallenge protocol at IoU $\geq 0.5$ via the
TrackEval toolkit~\citep{luiten2021hota,bernardin2008clear,ristani2016idf1}.
Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.3. As our primary ranking
metric we adopt HOTA, defined as the geometric mean of Detection Accuracy
(DetA) and Association Accuracy (AssA), since it explicitly disentangles
the two failure modes most relevant to spaceborne tracking: missed or
spurious detections of small targets (captured by DetA), and broken or
swapped trajectories under prolonged occlusion and inter-object
indistinguishability (captured by AssA). We additionally report DetA, AssA,
and Localization Accuracy (LocA) to enable diagnostic decomposition of
HOTA. To preserve comparability with prior spaceborne MOT
literature~\citep{yin2022satmtb,he2022airmot}, we also report the
legacy CLEAR metrics MOTA, MOTP, and IDsw, together with the Identity-based
IDF1 score, which complements HOTA by emphasizing long-range identity
preservation under a globally optimal one-to-one matching between
predicted and ground-truth tracks. Higher is better for all metrics
except IDsw, where lower is better.
\noindent\textbf{Experiment protocol.}

\noindent\textbf{Single Object Tracking.}

% \noindent\textbf{Inference protocol.}
\noindent\textbf{Inference protocol.} All trackers are evaluated under a strict
one-shot protocol: the ground-truth bounding box is injected only at frame 0 of
each video, and every subsequent frame is produced by the tracker's own temporal
propagation, with no further GT correction. Each video is partitioned at the
dataloader level into non-overlapping clips of $T{=}32$ frames (stride 1,
overlap 0); videos shorter than $T$ yield a single truncated clip. This clip
length keeps the protocol reproducible on modest hardware while remaining long
enough to exercise each tracker's temporal modeling. We use batch size 1
throughout---each forward pass consumes one clip of shape $[T,C,H,W]$---as every
tracker treats a clip as a stateful temporal sequence and trailing clips of
short videos have variable length. Clip partitioning is purely a dataloader
convenience and does not reset internal tracker state: SAM-family memory banks
and Siamese exemplar templates persist across clip boundaries via a carry-over
mechanism, in which the predicted bounding box at the last frame of clip $k$
serves as the prompt for the first frame of clip $k{+}1$. The ground truth is
never re-injected after frame 0, ensuring all trackers are compared under
identical supervision. If a tracker loses the target at a clip
boundary---producing no valid box to carry forward---all subsequent frames of
that video are recorded as empty predictions. All trackers run inference at the
native resolution of each dataset with no resizing applied.

\noindent\textbf{Prediction post-processing and rotated annotations.}
Predictions with confidence below $\tau{=}0.5$ are discarded prior to metric
accumulation. OOTB ground truth is provided as oriented bounding boxes (OBBs),
so we adapt the overlap computation to each tracker's native output:
mask-producing models (SAM~2, SAM~3, SAMURAI) are converted to OBBs and scored
against the GT under OBB IoU, whereas HBB-producing trackers (SiamRPN++,
OSTrack, ODTrack, LoRAT) are scored against the GT OBB's enclosing HBB under
standard HBB IoU. SatSOT and SV248S are annotated as HBBs natively and require
no such branching.

\noindent\textbf{Tracker variants.} We use the largest publicly released variant
of each tracker without any satellite-domain fine-tuning: SiamRPN++ with a
ResNet-50 backbone (layer 2--4 fusion, depthwise cross-correlation); OSTrack
with a ViT-B backbone (MAE-pretrained, 384$^2$ search region, candidate
elimination); ODTrack with the ViT-L baseline; LoRAT with a DINOv2 ViT-g/14
backbone and 378$^2$ search region; SAM~2 with the Hiera-L encoder (SAM~2.1
weights); SAMURAI reusing the same SAM~2.1 Hiera-L weights augmented by a Kalman
motion model for re-prompting; and SAM~3 with the official release and temporal
disambiguation enabled. For each tracker, we adopt the strongest variant
available in its official public release.

\section{Experiment results}

\taskhead{Single Object Tracking}

\subhead{Performance overview}
\label{sec:exp_sot_overview}

Following the selection rationale of Sec.~\ref{sec:model_selection},
Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results} reports the seven SOT methods on Space-Tracker-SOT
and on its tiny-object subset (median GT $\sqrt{\mathrm{area}} < 8$\,px), each
aggregated across SatSOT, SV248S, and OOTB via the macro-of-macro protocol of
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_eval_metrics}. We deliberately replace PR with P@5 in the tiny
column: when targets occupy fewer than a hundred pixels, the CLE-based PR curve
over $[0,50]$\,px saturates and ceases to separate trackers, whereas the strict
5-pixel criterion exposes the sub-pixel localisation gap that matters for
downstream geospatial use. Three observations follow.

\noindent\textbf{Foundation-pretrained trackers dominate; the classical lineage
is exhausted in this regime.} SAM 3 leads every column, LoRAT-g378 is second on
five of six metrics, and the SAM-2 family (SAM 2, SAMURAI) clusters immediately
behind. The Siamese baseline (SiamRPN++) and the spatiotemporal-fusion
transformer trackers (OSTrack-384, ODTrack) trail consistently. Notably,
ODTrack's spatiotemporal token propagation does not close the gap, indicating
that template--search correlation in feature space is fundamentally
information-starved when the target spans a handful of largely textureless
pixels. Web-scale pretraining appears to supply a scale-agnostic visual prior
that bespoke tracking heads cannot recover from satellite data alone.

\noindent\textbf{Progress on natural-scene SOT does not transfer to the tiny
regime.} The ranking is \emph{not} preserved between the two halves of
Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results}. OSTrack-384 (whole SR $0.320$) collapses to $0.139$
on tiny, falling \emph{below} SiamRPN++ ($0.153$) on all three tiny metrics ---
a tracker six years older. ODTrack, despite winning AAAI'24, is essentially tied
with SiamRPN++ on tiny SR ($0.159$ vs.\ $0.153$). Such inversions are unusual on
LaSOT or TrackingNet and constitute, in our view, the central diagnostic value
of Space-Tracker-SOT: improvements measured on natural video do not
automatically translate to spaceborne, sub-10-px targets, and may even regress.

\noindent\textbf{Tiny localisation is the bottleneck across the board.} Every
method loses between $26\%$ and $57\%$ of its whole-dataset SR when restricted
to the tiny subset. Even the strongest model, SAM 3, drops from $0.453$ to
$0.334$ in SR and reaches only $0.632$ in P@5 --- i.e., the best available
tracker still mislocalises more than one in three frames by over five pixels.
This headroom is the reason we release the tiny split as a first-class
evaluation target rather than a stress test.

\noindent\textbf{Takeaway.} The aim of this section is not to crown a winner. It
is to provide the community with a concrete, reproducible, satellite-specific
yardstick on which (i)~large-scale visual pretraining is rewarded, (ii)~progress
measured purely on natural-scene SOT benchmarks is at best partially correlated
with progress on spaceborne SOT, and (iii)~a sizeable accuracy gap, particularly
under the sub-8-pixel regime, remains open. We hope subsequent tracker design
will treat tiny, low-texture targets as a primary objective rather than a
secondary stress test.

Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results_whole} and Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results_tiny} in the
supplementary material.

\subhead{Performance under unified sequence attributes}
Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes} reports per-tracker SR / NPR / PR under our
unified attribute taxonomy (BC, IV, ROT, OCC, SOB, DEF). Each unified attribute
consolidates the corresponding native labels of every dataset that annotates it
(e.g.\ OCC = SatSOT \{POC, FOC\} $\cup$ SV248S \{STO, LTO, CO\} $\cup$ OOTB
\{PO, FO\}); the full mapping and the dataset-unique residue are reported in the

across the datasets that annotate the attribute.

\input{tables/SOT/whole_dataset/sot_attributes_table}

\noindent\textbf{Illumination, clutter, and deformation are no longer the bottleneck.}
The first cluster of unified attributes, IV, BC, and DEF, corresponds to
perturbations that the natural-video tracking community has worked on for over
a decade. On Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes}, SAM 3 reaches SR of 0.477 on IV,
0.466 on BC, and 0.568 on DEF, and the gap to the runner-up LoRAT-g378 is at
most three SR points. Even SAM 2 and SAMURAI sit within five points of LoRAT.
The ordering also holds on the tiny split (Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes_tiny}):
SR drops by 25 to 35\% on tiny BC and IV, but the per-tracker ranking remains
stable. This suggests that web-scale visual pretraining now supplies features
robust enough that local photometric perturbations and non-rigid deformation,
which dominated the early SOT literature, are no longer where the difficulty
lies at the scale of Space-Tracker-SOT. As a side note, DEF contains no tiny
instances by construction, since non-rigid deformation is only perceptible on
objects large enough to deform, which we take as a sanity check on the
taxonomy.

\noindent\textbf{Occlusion remains the central failure mode, and long-term
occlusion overturns the foundation-model ranking.}
OCC is the lowest-scoring unified attribute for every tracker we evaluated.
SAM 3, which leads on every other unified attribute by a comfortable margin,
drops to SR 0.364 under OCC, a 20-point fall from its DEF score. The
breakdown by occlusion duration in Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_occlusion_subtypes} is
where the picture becomes more interesting. On SatSOT POC and FOC, where
occlusion lasts only a few frames, SAM 3 leads cleanly and the natural-video
re-detection prior transfers without trouble. On the SV248S long-term and
continuous sub-types (LTO, CO; threshold 50 frames), however, LoRAT-g378
actually surpasses SAM 3 on SR, by 0.306 to 0.213 on LTO and 0.222 to 0.218
on CO. The same pattern shows up on tiny frames
(Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes_tiny_occlusion}), with LoRAT leading tiny LTO
at SR 0.254 against SAM 3's 0.142. Our reading is that the
re-detect-by-segment paradigm at the core of SAM 3 starts to degrade once
the target has been absent long enough for the segmentation prior to lose
its grounding, while LoRAT's transformer correlation, despite being weaker
overall, holds onto a more stable template across long gaps. Whether this
reflects a fundamental limitation of segmentation-based re-detection or
just an artifact of training-time occlusion distributions is, in our view,
the most consequential open question that Space-Tracker-SOT can be used
to study.

\noindent\textbf{Motion-cue ambiguity collapses tracking on tiny objects.}
The third diagnostic cluster, SOB, IM, and AM, targets the failure mode
discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:dataset_overview}: once appearance reduces to
a few-pixel blob, the surrounding motion field is the only cue left, and
that cue is itself ambiguous in spaceborne traffic scenes. On whole-data
SOB the foundation-model ranking is preserved (SAM 3 0.399, LoRAT 0.367).
But tiny IM is where the reasoning collapses most visibly. LoRAT-g378
drops from SR 0.386 on the whole split to 0.074 on tiny, an 81\% loss,
while ODTrack, which trailed badly almost everywhere else, holds 0.216
on tiny IM and finishes second only to SAM 3 (0.308). This is the only
attribute slice in our entire evaluation where ODTrack's temporal
mechanism produces a clear win against a more strongly pretrained
competitor. We read it as direct evidence that when target appearance is
uninformative \emph{and} surrounding motion is locally homogeneous,
appearance-only foundation models lose more than dedicated temporal
models do. Conversely, on AM, where surrounding motion is locally
heterogeneous (oncoming traffic), appearance and motion remain partly
complementary and SAM 3 recovers, with tiny AM SR back at 0.490. It is
also worth noting that motion blur (MB), historically a hard attribute
on natural video, is handled comparatively well even at tiny scale (SAM 3
0.383, LoRAT 0.262). The difficulty is no longer motion blur per se,
but whether motion provides discriminative information.

\noindent\textbf{Takeaway.}
Three findings emerge from the attribute analysis. First, the historical
priorities of the SOT literature, namely illumination, clutter, and
deformation, have largely transferred from natural to satellite video and
are not where the difficulty lies anymore. Second, occlusion remains the
dominant failure mode, and within occlusion the natural-video re-detection
paradigm that powers SAM 3 is overturned by simpler transformer
correlation under long, continuous occlusion. Third, the combination of
tiny scale and isotropic motion exposes a regime in which all
foundation-model trackers have substantial headroom, and in which
dedicated temporal modeling shows its only competitive advantage in our
study. We expect future tracker design targeting spaceborne settings to
treat these three regimes as primary evaluation slices rather than
incidental stress tests.

\section{Conclusion and Discussion}
We introduced Space-tracker, the first unified and curated "million-scale"
benchmark dataset designed for the detection and tracking of small targets in
spaceborne videos. By merging XX disparate datasets into a consistent COCO-style
framework, we provide a singular diagnostic platform to address the unique
geometric and radiometric constraints of the aerospace domain. Our primary
contribution lies in the unification of 12 fine-grained attributes, such as
sub-pixel target size and non-stationary backgrounds, enabling a rigorous
assessment of tracker failure modes across VNIR and thermal spectra. Extensive
benchmarking of seven state-of-the-art models reveals that while modern
architectures excel at motion modeling, sub-pixel accuracy and long-term
occlusion remain critical technical bottlenecks. Space-tracker establishes a
necessary performance baseline for advancing real-time monitoring applications
in high-stakes domains such as disaster management and environmental
conservation. Moving forward, we commit to the long-term maintenance of this
benchmark through an open evaluation server to ensure its continued utility as
an evolving standard for the research community.

\subsection{Dataset Overview}
The eight datasets that make up Space-tracker span a wide range of frame
resolutions, reflecting the heterogeneity of the underlying spaceborne and
aerial sources. SOT crops are highly heterogeneous: SatSOT and OOTB cover roughly

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:appendix_eval_metrics}

\subsubsection{Single Object Tracking}
Let $\{B_t^{\mathrm{pred}}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ and $\{B_t^{\mathrm{gt}}\}_{t=1}^{T}$
denote the predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes for a sequence of length
$T$, and let $\mathrm{IoU}_t$ and $\mathrm{CLE}_t = \lVert c_t^{\mathrm{pred}} -
c_t^{\mathrm{gt}} \rVert_2$ denote the per-frame intersection-over-union and
centre-location error (in pixels) respectively.
 
The third metric is presented as PR for the full benchmark and P@5 for the
tiny-object subset, since CLE-based PR saturates at sub-8-px scale where P@5 is
more discriminative.

\noindent\textbf{Success Rate (SR).} Following OTB~\citep{wu2015otb}, the
success plot reports the fraction of frames whose IoU exceeds a threshold $\tau
\in [0, 1]$,
\begin{equation}
S(\tau) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\mathrm{IoU}_t > \tau\right],
\end{equation}
and we report the area under this curve, $\mathrm{SR} = \int_0^1 S(\tau)\,
\mathrm{d}\tau$, which is mathematically equivalent to the mean IoU over all
frames. SR serves as our \emph{primary ranking metric} owing to its wide
adoption and scale-invariance in the IoU regime.
 
\noindent\textbf{Precision (PR).} The precision plot reports the fraction of
frames whose CLE falls below a pixel threshold $d$,
\begin{equation}
P(d) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\mathrm{CLE}_t < d\right],
\end{equation}
and we report $\mathrm{PR} = \frac{1}{50} \int_0^{50} P(d)\, \mathrm{d}d$, the
AUC normalized to $[0,1]$. We adopt the $[0, 50]$~px range originally defined by
OTB~\citep{wu2015otb} and used in the precision plots of
TrackingNet~\citep{muller2018trackingnet} and LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, rather
than the narrower $[0, 30]$~px range of OOTB~\citep{chen2024ootb}. The wider
range avoids saturation on the relatively larger targets present in OOTB while
keeping PR numerically comparable across our three datasets, which span
heterogeneous target-size distributions.
 
\noindent\textbf{Normalized Precision (NPR).} Raw CLE is sensitive to absolute
target scale, which conflates localization quality with target size---a
particular concern for satellite video where targets vary from a few to tens of
pixels. We therefore additionally report normalized precision, where CLE is
rescaled by the ground-truth box diagonal,
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}}_t \;=\; \frac{\lVert c_t^{\mathrm{pred}} - c_t^{\mathrm{gt}} \rVert_2}{\sqrt{(w_t^{\mathrm{gt}})^2 + (h_t^{\mathrm{gt}})^2}},
\qquad
\widetilde{P}(d) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}}_t < d\right].
\end{equation}
We report the AUC over $\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}} \in [0, 0.5]$, $\mathrm{NPR} =
\frac{1}{0.5} \int_0^{0.5} \widetilde{P}(d)\, \mathrm{d}d$, in line with the
post-2018 mainstream convention established by
TrackingNet~\citep{muller2018trackingnet}, LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, and
GOT-10k~\citep{huang2021got10k}. We deliberately deviate from OOTB's $[0, 1]$
range for two reasons: (i)~beyond $\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}} \approx 0.5$ the
curves of competitive trackers saturate and contribute little discriminative
signal, compressing the gap between methods; (ii)~on satellite video where
ground-truth diagonals are small, normalized errors above $0.5$ indicate
near-total localization failure, a regime already captured by SR at low IoU
thresholds. NPR serves as our \emph{secondary ranking metric}, and is
particularly informative for cross-scale comparisons. For direct comparability
with OOTB's published numbers, the legacy single-threshold value
$\mathrm{NP}_{0.5} = \widetilde{P}(0.5)$ is additionally reported in the
supplementary material.
 
\noindent\textbf{P@5.} As a diagnostic for fine-grained localization on small
targets, we report $\mathrm{P@5} = P(5)$, the fraction of frames with
sub-$5$-pixel centre error, following OOTB's adoption of $\mathrm{PR@5px}$ as
its primary precision metric. P@5 is reported alongside the three AUC-based
metrics but is \emph{not used for ranking}, since its meaning depends on
dataset-specific pixel scale and it provides finer-grained information than the
PR AUC alone.
 
\noindent\textbf{Aggregation.} All metrics are computed per sequence and then
averaged across sequences with equal weight. Formally, for any per-frame
quantity $m_{i,t}$ on sequence $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$ at frame $t \in
\{1,\dots,T_i\}$, the dataset-level score is
\begin{equation}
\overline{m} \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \frac{1}{T_i} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} m_{i,t} \right),
\end{equation}
treating each sequence as an independent evaluation unit regardless of its
length. This is the convention adopted by OTB~\citep{wu2015otb},
LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, and GOT-10k~\citep{huang2021got10k}, and matches the
protocol used by the original SatSOT, SV248S, and OOTB papers, ensuring our
reported numbers are directly comparable to published baselines on these
datasets. To provide a single-number summary across datasets, we additionally
report the equal-weight arithmetic mean of SR, NPR, and PR over the three
datasets, following the spirit of TrackingNet / LaSOT / GOT-10k aggregate
reporting; per-dataset results remain the primary unit of comparison. P@5 is not
aggregated across datasets, as its interpretation is pixel-scale dependent.

\subsubsection{Multi-Object Tracking Metrics}
\label{app:mot_metrics}

We evaluate MOT performance under the standard MOTChallenge
protocol~\citep{milan2016mot16} with an IoU matching threshold of $0.5$.
All metrics below are computed using the TrackEval
toolkit~\citep{luiten2020trackeval} from a single tracker output file per
sequence. We group the metrics into three families by their historical
origin and the aspect of tracking quality they emphasize.

\paragraph{HOTA family~\citep{luiten2021hota}.}
The Higher-Order Tracking Accuracy (HOTA) metric was introduced to
address the well-documented imbalance of CLEAR MOTA, which is dominated
by detection errors and underweights association quality. HOTA explicitly
factorizes tracking quality into a detection component and an association
component, and aggregates the result across multiple localization
thresholds.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HOTA} is defined as the geometric mean of detection and
    association accuracy, averaged over a range of localization
    thresholds $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, \ldots, 0.95\}$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{HOTA} = \frac{1}{19}\sum_{\alpha}
        \sqrt{\mathrm{DetA}_{\alpha} \cdot \mathrm{AssA}_{\alpha}},
        \quad \mathrm{HOTA} \in [0, 1].
    \end{equation}
    Geometric averaging penalizes trackers that excel along only one
    axis (e.g., strong detector but frequent ID switches), which we find
    informative for spaceborne settings where both small-object detection
    and long-range association are simultaneously challenging.

    \item \textbf{DetA} (Detection Accuracy) measures localization-level
    detection quality at threshold $\alpha$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{DetA}_{\alpha} =
        \frac{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}|}{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}| +
        |\mathrm{FN}_{\alpha}| + |\mathrm{FP}_{\alpha}|}.
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{AssA} (Association Accuracy) measures identity
    consistency by averaging, over all true-positive detections, the
    Jaccard similarity between the predicted track and the matched
    ground-truth track:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{AssA}_{\alpha} = \frac{1}{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}|}
        \sum_{c \in \mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}}
        \frac{|\mathrm{TPA}(c)|}{|\mathrm{TPA}(c)| + |\mathrm{FNA}(c)| +
        |\mathrm{FPA}(c)|},
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathrm{TPA}/\mathrm{FNA}/\mathrm{FPA}$ denote true-positive,
    false-negative, and false-positive associations relative to the
    matched GT track of detection $c$.

    \item \textbf{LocA} (Localization Accuracy) is the mean IoU over all
    true-positive matches and isolates pure spatial localization quality
    from detection and association errors.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{CLEAR family~\citep{bernardin2008clear}.}
We additionally report the classical CLEAR metrics to preserve direct
comparability with prior spaceborne MOT
benchmarks~\citep{yin2022satmtb,he2022airmot}, where CLEAR metrics remain
the de facto reporting standard.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MOTA} (Multi-Object Tracking Accuracy) aggregates all
    three error types into a single score normalized by the number of
    ground-truth boxes:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{MOTA} = 1 -
        \frac{\sum_{t}\bigl(\mathrm{FP}_{t} + \mathrm{FN}_{t} +
        \mathrm{IDsw}_{t}\bigr)}{\sum_{t}|\mathrm{GT}_{t}|},
        \quad \mathrm{MOTA} \in (-\infty, 1].
    \end{equation}
    MOTA can become negative when total errors exceed the number of GT
    boxes, which is not unusual in dense small-object settings.

    \item \textbf{MOTP} (Multi-Object Tracking Precision) is the average
    IoU over all true-positive matches and is numerically close to LocA,
    although the two differ slightly in their averaging conventions.

    \item \textbf{IDsw} (Identity Switches) counts the total number of
    times a ground-truth trajectory's matched predicted ID changes
    between consecutive frames; lower is better.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Identity family~\citep{ristani2016idf1}.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IDF1} is the F1 score computed under a globally optimal
    one-to-one matching between predicted and ground-truth identities,
    solved as a bipartite assignment over the entire sequence:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{IDF1} =
        \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{IDTP}}
        {2 \cdot \mathrm{IDTP} + \mathrm{IDFP} + \mathrm{IDFN}},
        \quad \mathrm{IDF1} \in [0, 1].
    \end{equation}
    Unlike AssA, which averages association quality locally per
    true-positive detection, IDF1 evaluates identity preservation at the
    \emph{trajectory} level and therefore complements AssA by being more
    sensitive to long-range identity consistency.
\end{itemize}

For all metrics except IDsw higher values indicate better tracking
performance. All metrics are class-agnostic by default; for multi-class
datasets (e.g., AIR-MOT, SAT-MTB) we additionally report per-class HOTA,
DetA, and AssA, computed by restricting both predictions and ground
truth to the relevant class before evaluation.

\subsection{Performance analysis w.r.t. object attributes}
The model performance w.r.t to different object attributes on the dataset SV248S
is shown in Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes_sv248s}. We can conclude the following
findings.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LoRAT-g378 dominates among pretrained trackers.}
    It ranks first on most attributes, with an especially commanding margin
    over other pretrained baselines on \emph{Natural Disturbance} (ND) and
    \emph{Slow Motion} (SM).
    \item \textbf{SAM~3 excels under occlusion.} On both
    \emph{Continuous Occlusion} (CO) and \emph{Long-Term Occlusion} (LTO),
    SAM~3 attains the top score across all three metrics. This suggests
    that the memory-bank mechanism of the SAM family confers a zero-shot
    advantage in recovering targets after prolonged occlusion -- a regime
    in which discriminatively trained trackers typically drift.
    \item \textbf{Counterexample on Background Cluster (BCL).}
    The pretrained LoRAT-g378 and OSTrack-384 outperform the zero-shot
    SAM~3 here, indicating that under the dense, visually homogeneous
    backgrounds characteristic of satellite video, supervised fine-tuning
    on remote-sensing data can still matter more than zero-shot
    generalization.
\end{itemize}
