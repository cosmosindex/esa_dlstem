\begin{abstract}
Spaceborne videos is a rapidly emerging field, yet it is
currently fragmented by disparate datasets that vary in quality, annotation
style, and sensory focus. We introduce Space-tracker, a unified, large-scale,
and multi-sensory benchmark dataset created through the strategic merging and
curation of \textit{nine} existing spaceborne and aerial datasets, such as SatSOT,
SV248S, and OOTB. Space-tracker addresses the ``paradigm gap'' in remote sensing
by providing a single point of access to multi-task annotations for object detection, single-object tracking, and multi-object tracking across multimodal video sequences. Our main contribution lies in the unification of labels and attributes, where we have harmonized diverse formats into a consistent COCO-style framework and applied 18 fine-grained attributes to classify challenges in spaceborne video benchmarks, such as tiny targets,
%($\sqrt{w \cdot h} < 8$ px, often featureless and near the sensor noise floor),
non-stationary backgrounds, long-duration
occlusion, and severe motion blur from low-frame-rate high-velocity acquisition. By benchmarking seven state-of-the-art single-object trackers and seven state-of-the-art multi-object trackers, including large Foundation Models, we provide a comprehensive evaluation, in particular for small objects.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\noindent\textbf{Motivation.}
The rapid advancement of space technology has brought us to a new era of Earth
observation, transitioning from static imagery to real-time dynamic monitoring.
The emergence of high-resolution video satellite constellations—such as Jilin-1 and SkySat has made it possible to gaze continuously at specific ground areas with high temporal resolution. This shift from static snapshots to continuous video streams provides rich spatiotemporal information that was previously inaccessible, representing one of the current frontiers in remote sensing.

The motivation for Space-tracker stems from the extreme scarcity and
fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne video data. While numerous specialized
datasets have been released—some focusing on specific platforms (e.g., Jilin-1),
others on single categories (e.g., ships), and some only on thermal
imagery—there is no unified standard that allows researchers to develop and
evaluate universal trackers. First, the "small object" problem remains the
primary bottleneck; targets in satellite videos are often reduced to
near-featureless bright spots, making texture-based discrimination nearly
impossible. Second, existing datasets use inconsistent annotation formats (HBB, OBB, or polygons) and diverse attribute definitions, which hinders the training of robust, generalizable models. %Third, there is a lack of multi-sensory consistency; many tracking pipelines assume perfect RGB conditions, yet real-world spaceborne applications require the ability to switch between visible and thermal modalities seamlessly. 
Space-tracker is motivated by the need to standardize these diverse streams into a single, high-precision
benchmark that enables the community to tackle the unique geometric and
radiometric challenges of the spaceborne domain under a unified protocol.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution of this paper.}
The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

\noindent \textit{Construction of a unified benchmark dataset.} We introduce the largest and most diverse curated benchmark to date for spaceborne video analytics. By strategically merging and cleaning eight disparate
datasets, we provide a single point of access to over 1 million annotated instances. This unified repository
addresses the extreme scarcity and fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne data, providing valuable resource for training robust geospatial models.

\noindent\textit{Harmonized Multi-Task and Multi-Sensory Annotations.} Unlike previous benchmarks limited to single tasks or sensors, Space-tracker supports a multi-task framework encompassing object detection, single-object tracking
(SOT), and multi-object tracking (MOT). We have harmonized all labels into a consistent COCO-style format and unified the attribute tagging system across the merged datasets. Furthermore, we integrate multimodal sequences, enabling researchers to develop trackers that generalize across visible and thermal spectra for all-weather monitoring.

\noindent\textit{Comprehensive Benchmarking of seven SOTA Models.} We provide an extensive evaluation of the current state of the art, benchmarking \hl{XX} backbone
models and \hl{XX} trackers across four major paradigms: traditional Discriminative Correlation Filters (DCF), deep Siamese networks, modern spatiotemporal Transformers, and Geospatial Foundation Models like SAM2. Our analysis offers
new insights into how these architectures handle small objects and long-term occlusions in non-stationary backgrounds.

\noindent\textbf{Generic Visual Object Tracking.} Siamese trackers cast tracking as similarity learning. SiamFC~\citep{bertinetto2016siamfc} introduces a fully convolutional Siamese framework for real-time tracking, followed by region-proposal and deeper-backbone variants that improve localization accuracy~\citep{li2018siamrpn,li2019siamrpn++}. SiamRPN++~\citep{li2019siamrpn++} remains a representative Siamese tracker and is used as a strong classical baseline in our experiments.

Transformer-based trackers later became dominant by modeling global context and long-range dependencies. \hl{STARK}~\citep{yan2021stark} performs direct box prediction, while OSTrack~\citep{ye2022joint} unifies feature extraction and relation modeling in a one-stream architecture. Recent methods further explore object discovery and large-scale tracking: ODTrack~\citep{zheng2024odtrack} incorporates object-discovery cues, and LoRAT-g378~\citep{lin2024tracking} represents a recent large-scale tracker with strong generalization. Although these trackers perform well on natural-video benchmarks, their robustness in spaceborne videos, especially for objects smaller than a few dozen pixels, remains underexplored.

Vision foundation models have also reshaped tracking. SAM~2~\citep{ravi2024sam} extends promptable segmentation to images and videos, SAMURAI~\citep{yang2026samurai} adapts SAM-style representations for tracking, and SAM~3~\citep{carion2026sam3} further advances foundation-model-based object understanding. These models generalize well in natural-image and natural-video domains, but spaceborne targets are often small, low-contrast, and weakly textured. Our benchmark therefore tests whether foundation-model-based trackers can generalize to the extreme small-object regime.

\noindent\textbf{Satellite Video Object Tracking.} Satellite video tracking
poses unique challenges, including extremely small object sizes, %\hl{often below $30$ pixels}, 
low contrast, weak appearance cues, complex platform motion, and
background clutter. Early satellite tracking approaches extend correlation
filters with motion modeling to improve robustness in satellite videos
\citep{lin2024motion}. Siamese-based methods have also been adapted to this
domain by incorporating high-resolution representations and motion fitting
strategies \citep{ruan2022deep, shao2021hrsiam}. More recently,
Transformer-based approaches have been explored to capture spatio-temporal
dependencies in satellite imagery \citep{lai2023target}.

Despite these advances, existing satellite-tracking studies are often evaluated
against limited benchmarks and do not fully meet the requirements of modern
spaceborne video understanding. In particular, existing benchmarks remain
insufficient in three aspects: they often focus on either detection or tracking
rather than a unified detection-and-tracking setting; many annotations rely on
axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack oriented bounding boxes (OBBs), which are
critical for objects with arbitrary orientations in aerial views; and their
scenario diversity, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions
remain limited. These limitations make it difficult to systematically evaluate
both classical trackers and recent foundation models in the extremely
small-object regime.

\noindent\textbf{Benchmarks for Satellite Detection and Tracking.} Existing
tracking benchmarks such as OTB~\citep{wu2013otb} and
LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}mainly focus on natural videos and do not capture the
unique challenges of satellite imagery. In the satellite domain,
SatSOT~\citep{satSOT2022} introduces a densely annotated benchmark for satellite
video single-object tracking, and SV248S~\citep{Li2022SV248S} further expands
the scale of satellite SOT evaluation. OOTB~\citep{CHEN2024212ootb} provides an
oriented tracking benchmark and highlights the importance of oriented bounding
boxes (OBBs) for aerial object localization. \hl{MOT benchmark datasets should be also mentioned?}

Despite these efforts, existing datasets exhibit several key limitations: (1)
extremely small object regime: most benchmarks do not explicitly small target
objects, which are common in spaceborne videos; (2) task fragmentation: datasets
are typically designed for either detection or tracking, lacking a unified
benchmark for joint evaluation; (3) lack of oriented annotations: most datasets
rely on axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack OBBs,
which are critical for accurately localizing objects with arbitrary orientations
in aerial views; and (4) limited diversity: existing datasets cover a limited
range of scenarios, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions,
restricting their ability to evaluate model generalization.

\noindent\textbf{Design principle.}
Space-tracker is constructed under multiple main principles:  1) Multi-modality: The dataset covers imagery from multiple platforms and constellations, including Jilin-1 sub-meter RGB, SkySat, and the International Space Station (ISS). %, and spans both visible--near-infrared (VNIR) and thermal infrared (TIR) modalities. 
2) Ease of access. To overcome the limitation of single-source benchmarks, we integrate eight heterogeneous datasets into a unified benchmark for aerospace analytics. 3) Space-tracker focuses on tracking small objects in complex scenes. We prioritize sequences in which targets occupy fewer than 8 pixels, where
limited spatial extent and weak visual content pose substantial challenges to tracking. The selected sequences also contain complex background structures over multiple spatial scales. These backgrounds may evolve at rates similar to the targets, introduce distractors, or cause frequent occlusions. %The dataset further covers diverse geographic regions, including \hl{Need to be checked: China, the United States, Libya, and Turkey, and varied temporal conditions}, with frame rates ranging from 3 to 30 FPS.

\noindent\textbf{Annotation unification and task coverage.} We standardize the source datasets through a unified preprocessing pipeline. Videos are decomposed into frame-level sequences, and metadata are organized under a common structure. Annotations from XML, JSON, and coordinate-only files are converted into a shared COCO-style format, with bounding boxes aligned to a common coordinate convention and class identifiers unified across datasets. The benchmark supports multiple tracking settings, but their annotation coverage differs by source. For SOT, each sequence contains one tracked target by construction, so the number of labels is at most the number of frames; absent or fully occluded targets are preserved according to the original conventions, e.g., \texttt{none} in SatSOT and invisibility flags in SV248S. For MOT, the available annotations describe dynamic objects in the scene, but static objects are not exhaustively labeled. Object detection is therefore only supported on a subset of SAT-MTB sequences, where static and dynamic objects are annotated together, and the available detection categories are limited to ship, plane, and train. We keep this distinction explicit in the benchmark protocol rather than treating all frame-level boxes as dense detection labels. After automatic conversion, annotations are manually inspected with an internal tool to correct misaligned boxes and add labels for previously unannotated large objects, such as cattle and specific vehicles.

\noindent\textbf{Definition of small-object.} Object scale is defined by the ground-truth bounding-box scale $s=\sqrt{\mathrm{area}}$. We adopt a pixel-based definition because the visual evidence available to a tracker is determined by the annotated image region rather than the physical size of the object or the sensor GSD. Based on the mean Success AUC (SR) and Normalised Precision AUC (NPR) of seven evaluated trackers on OOTB, SatSOT, and SV248S, using $2$-pixel bins over $s\in[0,32]$ px, tracker performance is consistently poor for $s<8$ px ($\mathrm{SR}<0.3$) and recovers to a moderate regime for $8\le s<16$ px ($\mathrm{SR}\approx 0.3\text{--}0.5$). We therefore define \emph{small} objects as $s<8$ px and \emph{small} objects as $8\le s<16$ px. These thresholds are kept identical across datasets for comparable size-stratified evaluation. Under this criterion, $63.1\%$ of all annotations in Space-tracker-SOT are small, and SV248S has an $80.3\%$ small-object ratio. The apparent sensitivity of SV248S at larger scales is mainly due to sparse bin support, with fewer than 200 frames per bin beyond $18$ px.

\noindent \textit{Data and annotation unification.}
We standardize all source datasets with a unified pipeline: videos are split into frame sequences, metadata are stored in a common structure, and XML, JSON, and coordinate-only annotations are converted to a shared COCO-style format with unified box conventions and class IDs. Annotation coverage differs by task and source. In SOT, each sequence contains one target, so labels are bounded by the number of frames; absent or occluded targets retain the original conventions, e.g., \texttt{none} in SatSOT and invisibility flags in SV248S. Converted annotations are manually inspected to fix box misalignment and add missing large-object labels, such as cattle and specific vehicles.

\noindent \textit{Attribute unification.} SatSOT, SV248S, and OOTB provide 11, 10, and 12 attributes for their annotations, respectively, with overlapping but inconsistent definitions. We align them into 18 fine-grained attributes. For example, Partial occlusion (PO) and full occlusion (FO) are mapped to a shared ``occlusion'' attribute for consistent attribute-level evaluation. The final taxonomy captures spaceborne-specific failure modes: Small Object (SO) is dominant; Similar Appearance (SA) and Dense Similarity (DS) occur when vehicles, lane markings, manhole covers, or bright pixels collapse into similar few-pixel blobs; Background Cluster (BCL) in SV248S denotes target-background merging for at least 10 frames. Motion ambiguity is represented by Isotropic Motion (IM), for nearby objects moving similarly, and Anisotropic Motion (AM), for strong opposite motion. Long-Term Occlusion (LTO) is caused by persistent structures such as overpasses, shadows, and canopies, with SV248S defining it as at least 50 occluded frames. Out-of-Normal (OON) aspect ratio captures elongated targets such as trains, while Natural Disturbance (ND) and Background Jitter (BJT) capture cloud, haze, and platform-induced jitter. These attributes characterize a regime where appearance and motion cues are often insufficient, enabling evaluation beyond aggregate accuracy.

\noindent\textit{Defining small object.}
We unified the small object attribute across all the dataset by defining it by the square root of the bounding box area \(s=\sqrt{\mathrm{area}}\). Across seven trackers on OOTB, SatSOT, and SV248S, evaluated with \(2\)-pixel bins over \(s\in[0,32]\) px, performance is poor for \(s<8\) px (\(\mathrm{SR}<0.3\)) and moderate for \(8\le s<16\) px (\(\mathrm{SR}\approx0.3\text{--}0.5\)). We therefore define \emph{small} objects as \(s<8\) px and \emph{medium-sized} objects as \(8\le s<16\) px, using fixed thresholds for comparable size-stratified evaluation. Under this definition, \(63.1\%\) of Space-tracker-SOT annotations are small, while SV248S reaches \(80.3\%\). SV248S sensitivity at larger scales mainly reflects sparse bin support, with fewer than 200 frames per bin beyond \(18\) px.

\section{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
\noindent\textbf{Model selection.}
\label{sec:model_selection}
To establish a robust performance baseline for Space-tracker, we adopt a
selection logic that includes several main methodology lines. Our goal is to evaluate
how different architectural evolutions, ranging from classical supervised pipelines to 
modern self-supervised foundation models, handle the unique constraints of small and even 
featureless targets in spaceborne video. We categorize our benchmarked methods into 
three primary methodological trajectories:

\noindent\textbf{Region Proposal and Detection-Based Tracking.} We select representative
models from the early deep learning era that established the foundation for
supervised tracking. This includes two-stage baselines like Faster R-CNN and
paradigms like Tracktor, which leverage region proposal networks (RPN) to
identify targets and subsequently associate them across frames. These models
serve as a classical reference for understanding the performance of explicit,
frame-by-frame supervised detection in satellite imagery.

\noindent\textbf{Spatiotemporal Fusion Models.} To address the limitations of independent
frame processing, we select architectures that explicitly model the time
dimension. This line includes spatiotemporal Transformers (e.g., HRLT, TATrans)
and joint detection and tracking (JDT) frameworks that couple appearance and
motion features during training. We also include unified fusion frameworks like
STAR, which integrates deep temporal features and physical motion priors (e.g.,
Kalman filters) to maintain identity consistency during long-term occlusions in
non-stationary backgrounds.

\noindent\textbf{Large Geospatial and Vision Foundation Models.} Reflecting the most
recent paradigm shift, we evaluate large-scale foundation models such as SAM 2
and DINO v3. These models are selected for their robust zero-shot generalization
and ability to leverage high-quality feature representations learned from vast
amounts of unlabeled data. By evaluating prompt-driven video segmentation and
tracking, we explore the potential of transfer learning to overcome the extreme
scarcity of labeled spaceborne data.

\subsection{Model Selection}
\label{sec:model_selection}
We organize our model selection by task. In the SOT setting the model is
given the target template in the first frame and only has to follow it, so
the comparison can focus purely on tracking quality. In the MOT setting
nothing is given for free: the tracker has to first decide what to track,
which means the choice of detector and the choice of associator are both
part of the design. The two settings call for different selection logic
and we cover them separately.

\noindent\textbf{Single Object Tracking.}
For SOT we deliberately spread our choices across several methodological
lines rather than pick only the most recent state of the art. What we want
to see is how architectural progress, from classical supervised pipelines
through to modern foundation models, copes with the unusual demands of
spaceborne video, where targets are often only a handful of pixels wide
and frequently lack any discriminative texture. We organize the
benchmarked methods into three trajectories.

\textit{Classical Siamese tracking.} SiamRPN++~\citep{li2019siamrpn++} is
kept as the canonical Siamese baseline that established correlation-based
tracking and has been the reference point for most papers that came
after. It gives us a clean classical reading against which the rest of
the table can be interpreted.

\textit{Transformer-based trackers.} We benchmark
OSTrack-384~\citep{ye2022joint}, ODTrack~\citep{zheng2024odtrack} and
LoRAT-g378~\citep{lin2024tracking} as three different answers to the same
question: how to exploit temporal information and large-scale pretraining
without breaking the latency budget that tracking demands.

\textit{Vision foundation models.} SAM~2~\citep{ravi2024sam},
SAMURAI~\citep{yang2026samurai} and SAM~3~\citep{carion2026sam3} reflect
the recent paradigm shift toward large foundation models with strong
zero-shot generalization. Benchmarking them lets us probe whether
features learned from massive natural image and video corpora actually
carry over to targets that are only a few pixels across.

\noindent\textbf{Two-stage Multi-Object Tracking.}
For MOT we deliberately restrict the benchmark to two-stage
tracking-by-detection pipelines rather than end-to-end MOT models, and we
want to spell out the reason here so it does not look like an arbitrary
choice. The end-to-end MOT direction, in which a single model jointly
detects, classifies and associates objects across frames, is promising
but remains essentially absent for spaceborne video. Strong recent
candidates such as MASA~\citep{li2024masa}, which adapts SAM into an
any-category tracker, or MOTIP~\citep{gao2024motip}, which formulates MOT
as in-context ID prediction without any heuristic association and is
currently SOTA on several natural-video benchmarks, are all trained on
natural-image and natural-video data. When we run them on satellite
footage without fine-tuning, they fail at the very first step: they
cannot reliably detect the objects of interest in our scenes, let alone
track and classify them. We observed the same pattern with
text-promptable foundation models. Grounding
DINO~\citep{liu2024groundingdino} essentially fails across every
satellite sequence we tried when asked to localize the target categories,
and SAM~3 only produces sensible masks
on the larger objects, with no way to tell static structures apart from
the moving instances we actually care about, which rules it out as a MOT
baseline. The lack of a credible end-to-end model for this regime is in
fact one of the motivations behind releasing Space-tracker; we hope the
dataset will make it easier for future work to train such models. For
the present benchmark, however, two-stage MOT is the only setting in
which we can give every method a fair chance, so that is what we focus
on.

A two-stage MOT pipeline needs a detector to feed it. As noted in
Section~3, only a subset of our sequences carries object-detection
annotations, and even there the labels only cover the \textit{ship},
\textit{plane} and \textit{train} categories. For the small moving
targets that typically appear as bare light spots in satellite imagery
and characterize the \textit{car} category (below roughly eight pixels
wide), the current SOTA detector is HiEUM~\citep{Xiao2024HiEUM}, which we
adopt directly. For
the remaining categories we train an in-house detector on the
object-detection training split of Space-tracker and run inference on
the held-out video sequences to produce the per-frame detections that
feed into the tracker. Both routes share the same downstream interface,
which keeps the comparison between trackers clean.

On top of these detections we evaluate two generations of trackers. The
first is the SORT family. SORT~\citep{bewley2016sort},
ByteTrack~\citep{zhang2022bytetrack}, OC-SORT~\citep{cao2023ocsort},
BoT-SORT and BoT-SORT-ReID~\citep{aharon2022botsort} are all
tracking-by-detection associators built on Kalman filtering and the
Hungarian algorithm, where each successor layers one targeted fix on top
of its predecessor, whether that is recovering low-confidence boxes,
observation-centric state updates, camera motion compensation, or
appearance ReID. They form the classical reading. The second generation
is represented by TrackTrack~\citep{kim2025tracktrack}, a more recent
learned tracker that goes beyond the SORT recipe and lets us measure how
much of the gap between classical association and modern learning-based
association actually transfers to satellite data.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
% 每一个精简到2-3句话
\subhead{Single Object Tracking}
We evaluate SOT trackers using four complementary metrics---Success Rate
(SR), Normalized Precision (NPR), Precision (PR), and P@5---chosen to align
with the dominant convention in modern generic-vision SOT
benchmarks~\citep{muller2018trackingnet,fan2019lasot,huang2021got10k} while
preserving the small-object diagnostic granularity required by satellite
video. Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.1. For overall
performance we report SR, NPR, and PR, capturing IoU-based overlap,
scale-normalized localization, and
pixel-level center accuracy respectively. For the \emph{small-object subset}---%
sequences with median GT side length $\sqrt{w_t^{\mathrm{gt}} h_t^{\mathrm{gt}}} < 8$~px
(the satellite analogue of COCO's $32$~px small-object
cutoff~\citep{lin2014coco})---we replace PR with P@5, since PR's $[0,50]$~px
integration range loses discriminative meaning once typical errors exceed the
target's full extent. Higher is better for all four metrics.

\subhead{Object Detection} We evaluate detectors using mean Average
Precision at IoU threshold $0.5$ ($\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$) as our primary ranking
metric, complemented by class-agnostic $\mathrm{AP}_{\text{overall}}$ and
operating-point Precision (P), Recall (R), and F1 at a fixed score threshold
of $0.5$. We deliberately adopt single-threshold $\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$ rather than 
the COCO-style $\mathrm{mAP}_{[.5:.95]}$ aggregate~\citep{lin2014coco}, since targets in
spaceborne video are typically smaller than $20\times 20$ pixels, where
strict IoU thresholds ($\geq 0.75$) tend to reflect sub-pixel localization
noise rather than genuine detector capability; single-threshold
$\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$ is also the prevailing convention in spaceborne
small-object detection~\citep{Li2023SATMTB,He2022TGraM}.
$\mathrm{AP}_{\text{overall}}$ merges predictions from all classes into a
single foreground category before constructing the PR curve, isolating
localization quality from the long-tailed class distribution typical of
satellite video. P, R, and F1 at a fixed threshold reveal
deployment-relevant behavior, such as over-confident false positives in
cluttered backgrounds and missed dim or sub-pixel targets, which
threshold-averaged AP tends to obscure. Higher is better for all five
metrics.
% Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.2.

\subhead{Multi-Object Tracking} We evaluate MOT performance using a
combination of HOTA-family, CLEAR-family, and Identity-family metrics
computed under the standard MOTChallenge protocol at IoU $\geq 0.5$ via the
TrackEval toolkit~\citep{luiten2021hota,bernardin2008clear,ristani2016idf1}.
Detailed metric definitions are in Appendix A.2.3. As our primary ranking
metric we adopt HOTA, defined as the geometric mean of Detection Accuracy
(DetA) and Association Accuracy (AssA), since it explicitly disentangles
the two failure modes most relevant to spaceborne tracking: missed or
spurious detections of small targets (captured by DetA), and broken or
swapped trajectories under prolonged occlusion and inter-object
indistinguishability (captured by AssA). We additionally report DetA, AssA,
and Localization Accuracy (LocA) to enable diagnostic decomposition of
HOTA. To preserve comparability with prior spaceborne MOT
literature~\citep{Li2023SATMTB,He2022TGraM}, we also report the
legacy CLEAR metrics MOTA, MOTP, and IDsw, together with the Identity-based
IDF1 score, which complements HOTA by emphasizing long-range identity
preservation under a globally optimal one-to-one matching between
predicted and ground-truth tracks. Higher is better for all metrics
except IDsw, where lower is better.

\subsection{Experiment protocol}
%
\subhead{Single Object Tracking} \minihead{Inference protocol} All trackers are evaluated under a strict
one-shot protocol: the ground-truth bounding box is injected only at frame 0 of
each video, and every subsequent frame is produced by the tracker's own temporal
propagation, with no further GT correction. Each video is partitioned at the
dataloader level into non-overlapping clips of $T{=}32$ frames (stride 1,
overlap 0); videos shorter than $T$ yield a single truncated clip. This clip
length keeps the protocol reproducible on modest hardware while remaining long
enough to exercise each tracker's temporal modeling. We use batch size 1
throughout---each forward pass consumes one clip of shape $[T,C,H,W]$---as every
tracker treats a clip as a stateful temporal sequence and trailing clips of
short videos have variable length. Clip partitioning is purely a dataloader
convenience and does not reset internal tracker state: SAM-family memory banks
and Siamese exemplar templates persist across clip boundaries via a carry-over
mechanism, in which the predicted bounding box at the last frame of clip $k$
serves as the prompt for the first frame of clip $k{+}1$. The ground truth is
never re-injected after frame 0, ensuring all trackers are compared under
identical supervision. If a tracker loses the target at a clip
boundary---producing no valid box to carry forward---all subsequent frames of
that video are recorded as empty predictions. All trackers run inference at the
native resolution of each dataset with no resizing applied.

\minihead{Prediction post-processing and rotated annotations}
Predictions with confidence below $\tau{=}0.5$ are discarded prior to metric
accumulation. OOTB ground truth is provided as oriented bounding boxes (OBBs),
so we adapt the overlap computation to each tracker's native output:
mask-producing models (SAM~2, SAM~3, SAMURAI) are converted to OBBs and scored
against the GT under OBB IoU, whereas HBB-producing trackers (SiamRPN++,
OSTrack, ODTrack, LoRAT) are scored against the GT OBB's enclosing HBB under
standard HBB IoU. SatSOT and SV248S are annotated as HBBs natively and require
no such branching.

\minihead{Tracker variants} We use the largest publicly released variant
of each tracker without any satellite-domain fine-tuning: SiamRPN++ with a
ResNet-50~\citep{he2016resnet} backbone (layer 2--4 fusion, depthwise
cross-correlation); OSTrack with a ViT-B~\citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}
backbone (MAE-pretrained~\citep{he2022mae}, 384$^2$ search region,
candidate elimination); ODTrack with the ViT-L baseline; LoRAT with a
DINOv2~\citep{oquab2024dinov2} ViT-g/14 backbone and 378$^2$ search
region; SAM~2 with the Hiera-L~\citep{ryali2023hiera} encoder (SAM~2.1
weights); SAMURAI reusing the same SAM~2.1 Hiera-L weights augmented by a
Kalman motion model for re-prompting; and SAM~3 with the official release
and temporal disambiguation enabled. For each tracker, we adopt the
strongest variant available in its official public release.

\subhead{Object detection} We train a Faster R-CNN~\citep{ren2015fasterrcnn} (ResNet-50~\citep{he2016resnet}\,+\,FPN, ImageNet-pretrained)
end-to-end on the SAT-MTB~\citep{Li2023SATMTB} train split for the three foreground classes (\emph{airplane}, \emph{ship}, \emph{train}).
Because spaceborne targets span a far narrower size range than natural-image objects (e.g., ships average $17$--$30$\,px on a side), 
we retune two components that are normally left at their COCO defaults: 
(i) the FPN \emph{anchor pyramid}, whose per-level sizes and aspect ratios we tighten to match the empirical bounding-box statistics of SAT-MTB, 
and (ii) the \emph{RPN and box-head matching thresholds}, which we relax from the standard $0.7/0.3$ and $0.5/0.5$ to be more permissive on positives, 
recovering small-object recall that is otherwise lost to anchor under-coverage. Models are validated every epoch on a held-out $18$-sequence val split, 
and the best checkpoint is selected by val mAP$_{50}$ with patience-$8$ early stopping; 
the $40$-sequence test split is strictly held out throughout development. 
For HiEUM~\citep{Xiao2024HiEUM} we evaluate the authors' released RsCarData-trained checkpoint off-the-shelf,
without any cross-dataset fine-tuning, in order to probe out-of-domain generalization of a video-native detector.
The resulting per-frame detections are additionally cached and reused as the shared input to all two-stage MOT trackers in \S\ref{sec:exp_mot_overall}.
Full training and inference details are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:protocol_det}.

\subhead{Multi-Object Tracking (Tracking-by-Detection)} For fairness, every tracker in this paradigm consumes the same
pre-cached frame-level detections, produced by HiEUM~\citep{Xiao2024HiEUM}
on the car-only datasets and by a Faster~R-CNN~\citep{ren2015fasterrcnn}
on the multi-class datasets. Differences in tracking metrics
therefore reflect association behavior rather than detector quality.
We keep each tracker's published MOT17/20 default hyperparameters
and depart from them in only three places, all motivated by the
small-object, station-keeping setting: lower first-stage IoU
thresholds, disabled global motion compensation, and per-tracker
score floors. The full configuration, evaluation splits, and ReID
setup are given in Appendix~\ref{appendix:mot-tbd}.

\section{Experiment results}

\subsection{Single Object Tracking}

\subhead{Overall performance}
\label{sec:exp_sot_overall}
On Space-Tracker-SOT, the seven trackers split into two clean tiers
along whether they are built on top of a foundation model. SAM 3
leads every column of Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results}, LoRAT-g378 is
second on five of six metrics, and the SAM 2 family (SAM 2, SAMURAI)
clusters immediately behind. SiamRPN++ and the two natural-video
transformer trackers (OSTrack-384, ODTrack) trail consistently, and
ODTrack's spatiotemporal token propagation does not narrow the gap.
When the target spans a handful of largely textureless pixels,
template-search correlation in feature space is information-starved,
and the scale of visual pretraining beats tracker design.

\subhead{Performance by object size}
\label{sec:exp_sot_size}
The whole-dataset ranking does not survive the move to tiny targets
(median $\sqrt{\mathrm{area}} < 8$\,px). Every tracker loses between
$26\%$ and $57\%$ of its whole-dataset SR, and the largest drops
fall on the natural-video transformer trackers. OSTrack-384
collapses from SR $0.320$ to $0.139$, finishing below SiamRPN++
($0.153$) on every tiny metric. ODTrack, despite winning AAAI 2024,
is statistically tied with SiamRPN++ on tiny SR ($0.159$ vs.
$0.153$). Even SAM 3 reaches only P@5 $0.632$, meaning the strongest
tracker still mislocalises more than one in three frames by over
five pixels. Inversions of this kind are unusual on LaSOT or
TrackingNet, and they are the diagnostic point we want this split
to make: progress measured on natural-scene SOT does not
automatically transfer to the sub-8-pixel regime and can regress.
The tiny split is therefore released as a first-class evaluation
target rather than a stress test.

Fig.~\ref{fig:sr_npr_vs_bbox_size} shows the same trend
continuously, with a sharp drop between two and four pixels below
which current trackers are not directly applicable.

\subhead{Performance under unified sequence attributes}
\label{sec:exp_sot_attributes}
Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes} reports per-tracker SR / NPR / PR
under our unified attribute taxonomy (BC, IV, ROT, OCC, SOB, DEF).
Each unified attribute consolidates the corresponding native labels
of every dataset that annotates it; the full mapping is reported in
the supplementary. Three findings emerge that the global ranking of
Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_results} hides.

\minihead{Classical perturbations are no longer the
bottleneck.}
On IV, BC, and DEF, SAM 3 reaches SR $0.477$, $0.466$, and $0.568$,
and the gap to LoRAT-g378 is at most three SR points. These are the
perturbations the natural-video tracking community has worked on for
over a decade, and web-scale pretraining apparently carries the
gains cleanly across to satellite frames.

\minihead{Long occlusion overturns the SAM 3 ranking.}
OCC is the lowest-scoring unified attribute for every tracker, and
SAM 3 drops 20 SR points from its DEF score on it. The duration
breakdown in Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_occlusion_subtypes} is the more
telling cut. On the short SatSOT sub-types (POC, FOC), SAM 3 leads
cleanly. On the SV248S long-term and continuous sub-types (LTO, CO;
threshold 50 frames), LoRAT-g378 actually surpasses SAM 3 on SR,
$0.306$ vs.\ $0.213$ on LTO and $0.222$ vs.\ $0.218$ on CO, and the
same pattern shows up on tiny frames. The likely mechanism is that
SAM 3's re-detect-by-segment design degrades once the target has
been absent long enough for the segmentation prior to lose its
grounding, while LoRAT's transformer correlation holds onto a more
stable template across long gaps.

\minihead{Tiny scale plus isotropic motion is where dedicated
temporal modeling earns its only win.}
Once appearance reduces to a few-pixel blob, surrounding motion is
the only cue left, and on tiny IM that cue is locally homogeneous.
LoRAT-g378 collapses from whole SR $0.386$ to $0.074$, an $81\%$
loss, while ODTrack, which trails almost everywhere else, holds
$0.216$ and finishes second only to SAM 3 ($0.308$). On tiny AM,
where surrounding motion is heterogeneous, SAM 3 recovers to SR
$0.490$. This is the only slice in our evaluation where
appearance-only foundation models lose more than dedicated temporal
models do.

Read together, the three findings reframe SOT difficulty in the
spaceborne regime: classical perturbations are essentially solved,
long occlusion and tiny-plus-isotropic motion are not, and which
tracker to pick depends on which slice you care about.

\subhead{Qualitative comparison}

\subhead{Multi-Object Tracking}

\minihead{Overall performance}
\label{sec:exp_mot_overall}
The most striking result in Tab.~\ref{tab:mot_results_combined} is that
SORT, the oldest tracker we evaluate, is also the strongest on the car
split. It wins five of eight columns and is second on a sixth, while
every tracker that adds score-gated rescue or appearance-based
association either matches it or fails outright. ByteTrack and BoT-SORT
both collapse to single-digit HOTA with IDsw on the order of $10^4$
to $10^5$. The reason is that HiEUM's score distribution is close to
binary, so the low-confidence rescue branch these methods rely on ends
up promoting noise into long ghost tracks rather than recovering missed
recalls. Adding a FastReID head on top of BoT-SORT recovers part of
the loss, but the result still trails plain SORT by 8.5 HOTA, because
car patches a few pixels across carry no usable appearance signal once
the head is shipped pre-trained on MOT17 pedestrians. TrackTrack sits
at the other extreme: it raises its initialisation thresholds enough
to be the only method with positive MOTA ($0.049$) and the lowest
IDsw ($666$), at the cost of the worst MT and ML in the panel.

The lesson mirrors what we report for SOT in Sec.~\ref{sec:exp_sot_overall}.
Progress on natural-video MOT since 2016 has not transferred to
spaceborne data, and a Kalman filter with IoU matching is the right
point of departure for new methods on this benchmark.

\minihead{Performance by object size}
\label{sec:exp_mot_size}
The car split and the larger-class split produce opposite rankings,
and the contrast is what we want the reader to take away. On cars
(typically a few pixels across, driven by HiEUM), motion-only SORT
wins five of eight columns, and every tracker that adds appearance or
score-gated association loses to it. On airplanes, ships, and trains
(an order of magnitude larger, driven by Faster R-CNN), the ranking
flips, and ByteTrack leads on HOTA, DetA, AssA, MOTA, and IDF1. The
IDsw blow-up that disqualified ByteTrack on cars ($37{,}364$) drops to
$406$ here, which tells us the car-side failure was a property of the
regime rather than of the tracker.

The point is straightforward. On spaceborne small objects, pixel-level
patches carry no useful appearance signal, and the detector's score
distribution is too narrow to give a second-pass rescue branch anything
to act on except noise. Neither of the two post-2016 ideas that drive
most modern MOT progress, learned appearance and score-gated
association, has anything to work with in this regime. A Kalman filter
with IoU matching turns out to be the right inductive bias, which is
why a 2016 tracker tops the table.

\section{Conclusion and Discussion}
We introduced Space-tracker, the first unified and curated "million-scale"
benchmark dataset designed for the detection and tracking of small targets in
spaceborne videos. By merging XX disparate datasets into a consistent COCO-style
framework, we provide a singular diagnostic platform to address the unique
geometric and radiometric constraints of the aerospace domain. Our primary
contribution lies in the unification of 12 fine-grained attributes, such as
sub-pixel target size and non-stationary backgrounds, enabling a rigorous
assessment of tracker failure modes across VNIR and thermal spectra. Extensive
benchmarking of seven state-of-the-art models reveals that while modern
architectures excel at motion modeling, sub-pixel accuracy and long-term
occlusion remain critical technical bottlenecks. Space-tracker establishes a
necessary performance baseline for advancing real-time monitoring applications
in high-stakes domains such as disaster management and environmental
conservation. Moving forward, we commit to the long-term maintenance of this
benchmark through an open evaluation server to ensure its continued utility as
an evolving standard for the research community.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:appendix_eval_metrics}
% TODO: define the SOT/MOT metrics used in the main paper -- SR / PR / NPR / P@5,
% MOTA / IDF1 / HOTA / AssA / DetA, and the macro-of-macro aggregation scheme.
\subsubsection{Single Object Tracking}
Let $\{B_t^{\mathrm{pred}}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ and $\{B_t^{\mathrm{gt}}\}_{t=1}^{T}$
denote the predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes for a sequence of length
$T$, and let $\mathrm{IoU}_t$ and $\mathrm{CLE}_t = \lVert c_t^{\mathrm{pred}} -
c_t^{\mathrm{gt}} \rVert_2$ denote the per-frame intersection-over-union and
centre-location error (in pixels) respectively.
 
The third metric is presented as PR for the full benchmark and P@5 for the
tiny-object subset, since CLE-based PR saturates at sub-8-px scale where P@5 is
more discriminative.

\noindent\textbf{Success Rate (SR).} Following OTB~\citep{wu2015otb}, the
success plot reports the fraction of frames whose IoU exceeds a threshold $\tau
\in [0, 1]$,
\begin{equation}
S(\tau) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\mathrm{IoU}_t > \tau\right],
\end{equation}
and we report the area under this curve, $\mathrm{SR} = \int_0^1 S(\tau)\,
\mathrm{d}\tau$, which is mathematically equivalent to the mean IoU over all
frames. SR serves as our \emph{primary ranking metric} owing to its wide
adoption and scale-invariance in the IoU regime.
 
\noindent\textbf{Precision (PR).} The precision plot reports the fraction of
frames whose CLE falls below a pixel threshold $d$,
\begin{equation}
P(d) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\mathrm{CLE}_t < d\right],
\end{equation}
and we report $\mathrm{PR} = \frac{1}{50} \int_0^{50} P(d)\, \mathrm{d}d$, the
AUC normalized to $[0,1]$. We adopt the $[0, 50]$~px range originally defined by
OTB~\citep{wu2015otb} and used in the precision plots of
TrackingNet~\citep{muller2018trackingnet} and LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, rather
than the narrower $[0, 30]$~px range of OOTB~\citep{chen2024ootb}. The wider
range avoids saturation on the relatively larger targets present in OOTB while
keeping PR numerically comparable across our three datasets, which span
heterogeneous target-size distributions.
 
\noindent\textbf{Normalized Precision (NPR).} Raw CLE is sensitive to absolute
target scale, which conflates localization quality with target size---a
particular concern for satellite video where targets vary from a few to tens of
pixels. We therefore additionally report normalized precision, where CLE is
rescaled by the ground-truth box diagonal,
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}}_t \;=\; \frac{\lVert c_t^{\mathrm{pred}} - c_t^{\mathrm{gt}} \rVert_2}{\sqrt{(w_t^{\mathrm{gt}})^2 + (h_t^{\mathrm{gt}})^2}},
\qquad
\widetilde{P}(d) \;=\; \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbbm{1}\!\left[\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}}_t < d\right].
\end{equation}
We report the AUC over $\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}} \in [0, 0.5]$, $\mathrm{NPR} =
\frac{1}{0.5} \int_0^{0.5} \widetilde{P}(d)\, \mathrm{d}d$, in line with the
post-2018 mainstream convention established by
TrackingNet~\citep{muller2018trackingnet}, LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, and
GOT-10k~\citep{huang2021got10k}. We deliberately deviate from OOTB's $[0, 1]$
range for two reasons: (i)~beyond $\widetilde{\mathrm{CLE}} \approx 0.5$ the
curves of competitive trackers saturate and contribute little discriminative
signal, compressing the gap between methods; (ii)~on satellite video where
ground-truth diagonals are small, normalized errors above $0.5$ indicate
near-total localization failure, a regime already captured by SR at low IoU
thresholds. NPR serves as our \emph{secondary ranking metric}, and is
particularly informative for cross-scale comparisons. For direct comparability
with OOTB's published numbers, the legacy single-threshold value
$\mathrm{NP}_{0.5} = \widetilde{P}(0.5)$ is additionally reported in the
supplementary material.
 
\noindent\textbf{P@5.} As a diagnostic for fine-grained localization on small
targets, we report $\mathrm{P@5} = P(5)$, the fraction of frames with
sub-$5$-pixel centre error, following OOTB's adoption of $\mathrm{PR@5px}$ as
its primary precision metric. P@5 is reported alongside the three AUC-based
metrics but is \emph{not used for ranking}, since its meaning depends on
dataset-specific pixel scale and it provides finer-grained information than the
PR AUC alone.
 
\noindent\textbf{Aggregation.} All metrics are computed per sequence and then
averaged across sequences with equal weight. Formally, for any per-frame
quantity $m_{i,t}$ on sequence $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$ at frame $t \in
\{1,\dots,T_i\}$, the dataset-level score is
\begin{equation}
\overline{m} \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \frac{1}{T_i} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} m_{i,t} \right),
\end{equation}
treating each sequence as an independent evaluation unit regardless of its
length. This is the convention adopted by OTB~\citep{wu2015otb},
LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}, and GOT-10k~\citep{huang2021got10k}, and matches the
protocol used by the original SatSOT, SV248S, and OOTB papers, ensuring our
reported numbers are directly comparable to published baselines on these
datasets. To provide a single-number summary across datasets, we additionally
report the equal-weight arithmetic mean of SR, NPR, and PR over the three
datasets, following the spirit of TrackingNet / LaSOT / GOT-10k aggregate
reporting; per-dataset results remain the primary unit of comparison. P@5 is not
aggregated across datasets, as its interpretation is pixel-scale dependent.

\subsubsection{Multi-Object Tracking Metrics}
\label{app:mot_metrics}

We evaluate MOT performance under the standard MOTChallenge
protocol~\citep{milan2016mot16} with an IoU matching threshold of $0.5$.
All metrics below are computed using the TrackEval
toolkit~\citep{luiten2020trackeval} from a single tracker output file per
sequence. We group the metrics into three families by their historical
origin and the aspect of tracking quality they emphasize.

\paragraph{HOTA family~\citep{luiten2021hota}.}
The Higher-Order Tracking Accuracy (HOTA) metric was introduced to
address the well-documented imbalance of CLEAR MOTA, which is dominated
by detection errors and underweights association quality. HOTA explicitly
factorizes tracking quality into a detection component and an association
component, and aggregates the result across multiple localization
thresholds.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HOTA} is defined as the geometric mean of detection and
    association accuracy, averaged over a range of localization
    thresholds $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, \ldots, 0.95\}$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{HOTA} = \frac{1}{19}\sum_{\alpha}
        \sqrt{\mathrm{DetA}_{\alpha} \cdot \mathrm{AssA}_{\alpha}},
        \quad \mathrm{HOTA} \in [0, 1].
    \end{equation}
    Geometric averaging penalizes trackers that excel along only one
    axis (e.g., strong detector but frequent ID switches), which we find
    informative for spaceborne settings where both small-object detection
    and long-range association are simultaneously challenging.

    \item \textbf{DetA} (Detection Accuracy) measures localization-level
    detection quality at threshold $\alpha$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{DetA}_{\alpha} =
        \frac{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}|}{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}| +
        |\mathrm{FN}_{\alpha}| + |\mathrm{FP}_{\alpha}|}.
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{AssA} (Association Accuracy) measures identity
    consistency by averaging, over all true-positive detections, the
    Jaccard similarity between the predicted track and the matched
    ground-truth track:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{AssA}_{\alpha} = \frac{1}{|\mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}|}
        \sum_{c \in \mathrm{TP}_{\alpha}}
        \frac{|\mathrm{TPA}(c)|}{|\mathrm{TPA}(c)| + |\mathrm{FNA}(c)| +
        |\mathrm{FPA}(c)|},
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathrm{TPA}/\mathrm{FNA}/\mathrm{FPA}$ denote true-positive,
    false-negative, and false-positive associations relative to the
    matched GT track of detection $c$.

    \item \textbf{LocA} (Localization Accuracy) is the mean IoU over all
    true-positive matches and isolates pure spatial localization quality
    from detection and association errors.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{CLEAR family~\citep{bernardin2008clear}.}
We additionally report the classical CLEAR metrics to preserve direct
comparability with prior spaceborne MOT
benchmarks~\citep{Li2023SATMTB,He2022TGraM}, where CLEAR metrics remain
the de facto reporting standard.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MOTA} (Multi-Object Tracking Accuracy) aggregates all
    three error types into a single score normalized by the number of
    ground-truth boxes:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{MOTA} = 1 -
        \frac{\sum_{t}\bigl(\mathrm{FP}_{t} + \mathrm{FN}_{t} +
        \mathrm{IDsw}_{t}\bigr)}{\sum_{t}|\mathrm{GT}_{t}|},
        \quad \mathrm{MOTA} \in (-\infty, 1].
    \end{equation}
    MOTA can become negative when total errors exceed the number of GT
    boxes, which is not unusual in dense small-object settings.

    \item \textbf{MOTP} (Multi-Object Tracking Precision) is the average
    IoU over all true-positive matches and is numerically close to LocA,
    although the two differ slightly in their averaging conventions.

    \item \textbf{IDsw} (Identity Switches) counts the total number of
    times a ground-truth trajectory's matched predicted ID changes
    between consecutive frames; lower is better.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Identity family~\citep{ristani2016idf1}.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IDF1} is the F1 score computed under a globally optimal
    one-to-one matching between predicted and ground-truth identities,
    solved as a bipartite assignment over the entire sequence:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{IDF1} =
        \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{IDTP}}
        {2 \cdot \mathrm{IDTP} + \mathrm{IDFP} + \mathrm{IDFN}},
        \quad \mathrm{IDF1} \in [0, 1].
    \end{equation}
    Unlike AssA, which averages association quality locally per
    true-positive detection, IDF1 evaluates identity preservation at the
    \emph{trajectory} level and therefore complements AssA by being more
    sensitive to long-range identity consistency.
\end{itemize}

For all metrics except IDsw higher values indicate better tracking
performance. All metrics are class-agnostic by default; for multi-class
datasets (e.g., AIR-MOT, SAT-MTB) we additionally report per-class HOTA,
DetA, and AssA, computed by restricting both predictions and ground
truth to the relevant class before evaluation.

\subsection{Object detection protocol}
\label{app:protocol_det}
\minihead{Faster R-CNN}
We use the \texttt{torchvision} \texttt{fasterrcnn\_resnet50\_fpn} (V1) implementation with three trainable backbone stages and a $C{=}4$ classification head (3 foreground + background). The FPN anchor pyramid is set to sizes $\{8,16\},\{24,48\},\{64,96\},\{128,192\},\{256,384\}$ with aspect ratios $\{0.5,1,2\}$, and matching thresholds are relaxed to $\tau^{\text{rpn}}_{\text{fg/bg}}{=}0.5/0.2$ and $\tau^{\text{box}}_{\text{fg/bg}}{=}0.4/0.3$. RPN proposals are capped at $4000/2000$ pre/post-NMS during training. Inputs are resized by \texttt{GeneralizedRCNNTransform} to short side $1024$, long side $\le 1333$. The augmentation pipeline is $\texttt{PadIfNeeded}(1024)\!\to\!\texttt{RandomCrop}(1024)\!\to\!\text{HFlip}(p{=}0.5)\!\to\!\text{VFlip}(p{=}0.2)\!\to\!\texttt{RandomBrightnessContrast}(\pm 15\%, p{=}0.3)$, with bounding-box \emph{min\_visibility}$\,=\,0.1$. Optimization uses AdamW (lr $5{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$), $3$-epoch linear warm-up followed by cosine annealing for up to $30$ epochs, batch size $4$, and $16$-bit mixed precision. At inference we use \emph{score\_thresh}$\,{=}\,0.05$, \emph{nms\_thresh}$\,{=}\,0.5$, and at most $300$ detections per image; for the MOT detection cache the long-side cap is raised to $2304$\,px to preserve native-resolution recall.

\minihead{HiEUM}
We use the authors' released checkpoint \texttt{model\_best.pth} trained on RsCarData; no cross-dataset fine-tuning is performed. The architectural hyperparameters are baked into the checkpoint and held fixed at $L{=}3$ transformer layers, clip length $T{=}20$, input size $1024{\times}1024$. Clips are sampled with stride $1$ and batch size $1$. Detection caching uses \emph{score\_thresh}$\,{=}\,0.05$, NMS IoU $0.1$, and at most $128$ detections per frame. Following the original protocol, matches are scored by centroid Euclidean distance with a $5$\,px threshold; the reported best-F1 number is obtained by sweeping the score threshold over $\{0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.32, 0.34, 0.35\}$.

\section{MOT (Tracking-by-Detection): Full Protocol}
\label{appendix:mot-tbd}

\paragraph{Cached detections and evaluation splits.}
We cache detections once per dataset and serve them from disk to
every tracker. RsCarData, SAT-MTB-car, and SDM-Car use
HiEUM~\citep{Xiao2024HiEUM}. SAT-MTB-\{airplane, ship, train\}, VISO
non-car, and AIR-MOT-100 use a Faster~R-CNN~\citep{ren2015fasterrcnn}
trained on the SAT-MTB \texttt{train} split (84 sequences) with
checkpoint selection on \texttt{val} (18 sequences). To rule out
train/test leakage, we evaluate tracking only on the SAT-MTB
\texttt{test} split (40 sequences) and on the full sequence sets
of VISO non-car and AIR-MOT-100, since the detector never saw
either of the latter two during training. Each cached frame is
serialized as a JSON record of \texttt{\{boxes, scores\}} for the
car half and \texttt{\{boxes, scores, labels\}} for the multi-class
half.

\paragraph{Multi-class handling.}
On the multi-class half, identities are not allowed to cross
categories. We instantiate one tracker per
(\emph{video}, \emph{class}) pair and aggregate per-class results
only at the metric-reporting stage.

\paragraph{Appearance features (ReID trackers).}
BoT-SORT-ReID and TrackTrack additionally read a parallel
\texttt{.npz} file with $L_2$-normalized 2048-D embeddings from
FastReID SBS-S50~\citep{he2023fastreid}, using its MOT17 pedestrian
weights without any spaceborne fine-tuning. This is intentional.
The cross-domain gap between pedestrian appearance models and
overhead small targets is part of what we want this benchmark to
measure, so the BoT-SORT-ReID and TrackTrack numbers should be read
as a transfer result rather than an upper bound.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter deviations from MOT17/20 defaults.}
\emph{(i) IoU thresholds.}~We lower the first-stage IoU threshold
to $0.1$ in SORT and OC-SORT. Targets in our benchmark span 5 to
15~px; at this scale a one-pixel localization error can drop IoU
below the conventional $0.3$ cutoff. A val-subset sweep confirmed
that values in $[0.1, 0.3]$ are stable and that $0.3$ materially
under-associates.
\emph{(ii) Camera-motion compensation.}~We disable BoT-SORT's GMC
module (\texttt{cmc\_method=none}). All platforms in our benchmark
are station-keeping or geostationary; inter-frame ego-motion is
negligible, and enabling GMC in this regime adds variance without
measurable gain.
\emph{(iii) Detection score floors.}~Score floors are calibrated so
that each tracker operates inside its intended score-cascade
regime: $0.25$ for SORT (single-stage IoU only), $0.10$ for
ByteTrack, BoT-SORT, BoT-SORT-ReID, and TrackTrack (which preserves
the low-score recovery cascade), and $0.0$ for OC-SORT, whose
internal \texttt{det\_thresh}$=0.30$ already filters detections.
All other hyperparameters retain their published defaults. The full
configuration appears in Table~\ref{tab:mot-hparams}.

\subsection{Performance analysis w.r.t. object attributes}
The model performance w.r.t to different object attributes on the dataset SV248S
is shown in Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes_sv248s}. We can conclude the following
findings.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LoRAT-g378 dominates among pretrained trackers.}
    It ranks first on most attributes, with an especially commanding margin
    over other pretrained baselines on \emph{Natural Disturbance} (ND) and
    \emph{Slow Motion} (SM).
    \item \textbf{SAM~3 excels under occlusion.} On both
    \emph{Continuous Occlusion} (CO) and \emph{Long-Term Occlusion} (LTO),
    SAM~3 attains the top score across all three metrics. This suggests
    that the memory-bank mechanism of the SAM family confers a zero-shot
    advantage in recovering targets after prolonged occlusion -- a regime
    in which discriminatively trained trackers typically drift.
    \item \textbf{Counterexample on Background Cluster (BCL).}
    The pretrained LoRAT-g378 and OSTrack-384 outperform the zero-shot
    SAM~3 here, indicating that under the dense, visually homogeneous
    backgrounds characteristic of satellite video, supervised fine-tuning
    on remote-sensing data can still matter more than zero-shot
    generalization.
\end{itemize}
