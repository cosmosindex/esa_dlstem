\begin{abstract}
Remote sensing with spaceborne videos is a rapidly emerging field, yet the field is currently fragmented by disparate datasets that vary in quality, annotation style, and sensory focus. We introduce Space-tracker, a unified, large-scale, and multi-sensory benchmark dataset created through the strategic merging and curation of eight existing spaceborne and aerial datasets, such as SatSOT, SV248S, and OOTB. Space-tracker addresses the "paradigm gap" in remote sensing by providing a single point of access to multi-task annotations for object detection, single-object tracking, and multi-object tracking across multimodal video sequences (visible to near-inferad and thermal infared). Our main contribution lies in the unification of labels and attributes, where we have harmonized diverse formats into a consistent COCO-style framework and applied 12 fine-grained attributes to classify challenges like tiny targets (often < 8 pixels), non-stationary backgrounds, and motion blur. By benchmarking seven state-of-the-art models, including Geospatial Foundation Models, we provide the most comprehensive evaluation to date on the tracking of featureless, sub-pixel targets from space.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Motivation}
The rapid advancement of space technology has brought us to a new era of Earth observation, transitioning from static imagery to real-time dynamic monitoring. The emergence of high-resolution video satellite constellations—such as Jilin-1 and SkySat has made it possible to gaze continuously at specific ground areas with high temporal resolution. This shift from static snapshots to continuous video streams provides a wealth of spatiotemporal information that was previously inaccessible, representing one of the current frontiers in remote sensing.

The motivation for Space-tracker stems from the extreme scarcity and fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne video data. While numerous specialized datasets have been released—some focusing on specific platforms (e.g., Jilin-1), others on single categories (e.g., ships), and some only on thermal imagery—there is no unified standard that allows researchers to develop and evaluate universal trackers.
First, the "small object" problem remains the primary bottleneck; targets in satellite videos are often reduced to near-featureless bright spots, making texture-based discrimination nearly impossible. Second, existing datasets use inconsistent annotation formats (HBB, OBB, or polygons) and diverse attribute definitions, which hinders the training of robust, generalizable models like Transformers. Third, there is a lack of multi-sensory consistency; many tracking pipelines assume perfect RGB conditions, yet real-world spaceborne applications require the ability to switch between visible and thermal modalities seamlessly. Space-tracker is motivated by the need to standardize these diverse streams into a single, high-precision benchmark that enables the community to tackle the unique geometric and radiometric challenges of the spaceborne domain under a unified protocol.


\subsection{Related work}
\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Generic Visual Object Tracking.}
%Early visual tracking methods are dominated by correlation-filter-based approaches that exploit the circulant structure for efficient learning. \citet{henriques2015kcf} propose Kernelized Correlation Filters (KCF), achieving high efficiency but relying on local appearance modeling, which is brittle in the presence of background clutter and becomes unreliable when the target is extremely small.

Siamese-based trackers reformulate tracking as a similarity learning problem. \citet{bertinetto2016siamfc} introduce a fully convolutional Siamese framework for real-time tracking, while subsequent works improve localization accuracy through region proposal networks and deeper backbones \citep{li2018siamrpn, li2019siamrpn++}. In particular, SiamRPN++~\citep{li2019siamrpn++} remains a representative Siamese tracker and serves as a strong classical baseline in our experiments.

More recently, Transformer-based trackers have become dominant by modeling global context and long-range dependencies. STARK~\citep{yan2021stark} formulates tracking as direct box prediction, while OSTrack~\citep{ye2022joint} unifies feature extraction and relation modeling in a one-stream Transformer architecture. Beyond conventional template-matching paradigms, recent trackers have further explored object discovery and large-scale tracking architectures. ODTrack~\citep{zheng2024odtrack} introduces object-discovery cues for robust tracking, and LoRAT-g378~\citep{lin2024tracking} represents a recent large-scale tracker with strong generalization capability. These trackers achieve strong performance on natural video benchmarks, but they are primarily designed for scenes with targets that are relatively large, visually distinctive, and well-textured. Their robustness under spaceborne conditions, especially for objects smaller than a few dozen pixels, remains underexplored.

Recent advances in vision foundation models further reshape the tracking landscape. SAM~2~\citep{ravi2024sam} extends promptable segmentation to images and videos, while SAMURAI~\citep{yang2026samurai} adapts SAM-style representations for robust visual tracking. More recently, SAM~3~\citep{carion2026sam3} further advances foundation-model-based object understanding. These models demonstrate strong generalization across natural-image and natural-video domains, but are primarily trained on objects with clear boundaries and sufficient spatial extent. In spaceborne videos, targets are often extremely small, low-contrast, and sparsely textured, making promptable segmentation and foundation-model-based tracking substantially more challenging. Therefore, evaluating these models on our benchmark provides a direct test of whether modern foundation models can generalize to the extremely small-object regime.

\paragraph{Satellite Video Object Tracking.}
Satellite video tracking poses unique challenges, including extremely small object sizes, often below $30$ pixels, low contrast, weak appearance cues, complex platform motion, and background clutter. Early satellite tracking approaches extend correlation filters with motion modeling to improve robustness in satellite videos \citep{lin2024motion}. Siamese-based methods have also been adapted to this domain by incorporating high-resolution representations and motion fitting strategies \citep{ruan2022deep, shao2021hrsiam}. More recently, Transformer-based approaches have been explored to capture spatio-temporal dependencies in satellite imagery \citep{lai2023target}. 

Despite these advances, existing satellite-tracking studies are often evaluated against limited benchmarks and do not fully meet the requirements of modern spaceborne video understanding. In particular, existing benchmarks remain insufficient in three aspects: they often focus on either detection or tracking rather than a unified detection-and-tracking setting; many annotations rely on axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack oriented bounding boxes (OBBs), which are critical for objects with arbitrary orientations in aerial views; and their scenario diversity, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions remain limited. These limitations make it difficult to systematically evaluate both classical trackers and recent foundation models in the extremely small-object regime.

\paragraph{Benchmarks for Satellite Detection and Tracking.}
Existing tracking benchmarks such as OTB~\citep{wu2013otb} and LaSOT~\citep{fan2019lasot}mainly focus on natural videos and do not capture the unique challenges of satellite imagery. In the satellite domain, SatSOT~\citep{satSOT2022} introduces a densely annotated benchmark for satellite video single-object tracking, and SV248S~\citep{Li2022SV248S} further expands the scale of satellite SOT evaluation. OOTB~\citep{CHEN2024212ootb} provides an oriented tracking benchmark and highlights the importance of oriented bounding boxes (OBBs) for aerial object localization.

Despite these efforts, existing datasets exhibit several key limitations: 
(1) extremely small object regime: most benchmarks do not explicitly 
small target objects, which are common in spaceborne videos; (2) task fragmentation: datasets are typically designed for either detection or tracking, lacking a unified benchmark for joint evaluation; (3) lack of oriented annotations: most datasets rely on axis-aligned bounding boxes and lack-oriented bounding boxes (OBBs), which are critical for accurately localizing objects with arbitrary orientations in aerial views; and (4) limited diversity: existing datasets cover a limited range of scenarios, object categories, motion patterns, and imaging conditions, restricting their ability to evaluate model generalization.
\subsection{Contribution of this paper}
The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\textbf{Construction of Space-tracker, a unified  benchmark dataset}: We introduce the largest and most diverse curated benchmark to date for spaceborne video analytics. By strategically merging and cleaning eight disparate datasets—including SatSOT, VISO, SV248S, OOTB, and BIRDSAI—we provide a single point of access to over 1 million annotated instances. This unified repository addresses the extreme scarcity and fragmentation of high-quality spaceborne data, providing valuable resource for training robust geospatial models.

\textbf{Harmonized Multi-Task and Multi-Sensory Annotations}: Unlike previous benchmarks limited to single tasks or sensors, Space-tracker supports a multi-task framework encompassing object detection, single-object tracking (SOT), and multi-object tracking (MOT). We have harmonized all labels into a consistent COCO-style format and unified the attribute tagging system across the merged datasets. Furthermore, we integrate multimodal sequences (VNIR and TIR), enabling researchers to develop trackers that generalize across visible and thermal spectra for all-weather monitoring

\textbf{Comprehensive Benchmarking of seven SOTA Models}: We provide an extensive evaluation of the current state of the art, benchmarking XX backbone models and XX trackers across four major paradigms: traditional Discriminative Correlation Filters (DCF), deep Siamese networks, modern spatiotemporal Transformers, and Geospatial Foundation Models like SAM2. Our analysis offers new insights into how these architectures handle small objects and long-term occlusions in non-stationary backgrounds.


\section{Our Dataset}
\subsection{Design principle}
The construction of Space-tracker is guided by the principle of multi-sensory and multi-platform diversity to overcome the limitations of existing single-source datasets. We strategically selected and merged XX disparate datasets, including SatSOT, VISO, SV248S, OOTB, and BIRDSAI, to provide a comprehensive view of aerospace analytics. Our key design principles are:

\textbf{Diverse Sensor Integration}: The benchmark incorporates imagery from a wide range of constellations, including Jilin-1 (sub-meter RGB), SkySat, and the International Space Station (ISS).

\textbf{Multimodal Modality}: Unlike existing benchmarks limited to visible light, Space-tracker integrates multimodal sequences spanning both visible–near-infrared (VNIR) and thermal infrared (TIR) spectra.

\textbf{Focus on the "Small Object" Problem}: Selection was prioritized for sequences where targets occupy fewer than 10 pixels, addressing the acute challenges of sub-pixel motion and limited spatial features in satellite video.

\textbf{Background Complexity}: Sequences were chosen for their complex background structures over multiple length scales, which can evolve at similar rates to the targets or cause frequent occlusions.

\textbf{Geographic and Temporal Variety}: The dataset covers diverse global regions (e.g., China, USA, Libya, Turkey) and varying temporal conditions, with frame rates ranging from 3 to 30 FPS.
    


\subsection{Pre-processing workflow}

To transform fragmented sources into a robust benchmark, we implemented a rigorous processing pipeline. This includes the follows steps.

\textbf{Annotation Aggregation and Unification}: We harmonized disparate label formats (XML, JSON, and simple coordinate files) into a consistent COCO-style classification and detection framework. All bounding box definitions were aligned to a unified coordinate convention and class indexing system.

\textbf{Label Attribution Harmonization and Enrichment}: We harmonized these diverse systems into a single framework of 12 fine-grained attributes tailored to the specific challenges of spaceborne video analytics [Previous conversation history]. This involved mapping original labels—such as "Partial Occlusion" (PO) and "Full Occlusion" (FO)—into a unified convention to ensure that trackers are evaluated under consistent definitions of difficulty.

%>>TODO: did we do subsampling?<<%Data Cleaning and Subsampling: All videos were decomposed into individual image frames to ensure a consistent data structure. Following the "scaling law" for robust training, we subsampled large datasets (exceeding XX samples) to ensure the benchmark remains representative of typical downstream tasks where data is scarce.

\textbf{Manual Refinement}: We utilized an internally developed tool to manually correct misaligned targets or add labels for unannotated large objects (e.g., cattle or specific vehicles), ensuring high-quality ground truth across all merged sources.

\textbf{Object size regime definition}:
In addition to the attributes introduced above, we further define the notions of \emph{small object} and \emph{tiny object} based on the empirical behaviour of all evaluated trackers. A distinctive characteristic of remote-sensing datasets is that objects which appear large in the physical world can become vanishingly small on the image plane: a car travelling at high speed may occupy no more than a faint point of light in a satellite video frame. Regardless of the object's real-world identity, the only information available to a deep-learning tracker is the visual evidence contained within the annotated bounding box, so its pixel size---rather than the object's physical extent---is what determines tracking difficulty. A bounding box of size $s$ pixels presents the same visual evidence to a tracker regardless of the sensor's GSD, which motivates a unified, pixel-based criterion applicable across datasets with heterogeneous GSDs.

We partition every frame by the ground-truth bounding-box scale $s=\sqrt{\mathrm{area}}$ and examine how tracker performance varies with $s$.
Figure~\ref{fig:sr_npr_vs_bbox_size} plots the mean Success AUC (SR) and Normalised Precision AUC (NPR) of the seven evaluated trackers against $s$ on OOTB, SatSOT, and SV248S, with $2$-pixel bins over $s\in[0,32]\,$px.
Across all three datasets the curves share a consistent shape: trackers perform poorly ($\mathrm{SR}<0.3$) for $s<8\,$px, and recover to a moderate regime ($\mathrm{SR}\approx 0.3\text{--}0.5$) between $8$ and $16\,$px.
Guided by these common inflection points, we adopt a unified, dataset-agnostic partition---\emph{tiny} ($s<8\,$px) and \emph{small} ($8\le s<16\,$px)---which we use throughout the remainder of the paper.
Thresholds are kept identical across datasets so that size-stratified results are directly comparable; the apparent sensitivity of SV248S at larger scales reflects sparse bin support ($<200$ frames per bin beyond $18\,$px) rather than a genuinely different size--difficulty relationship.

\section{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
\subsection{Model selection}
To establish a robust performance baseline for Space-tracker, we adopt a selection logic that includes diverse methodology lines. Our goal is to evaluate how different architectural evolutions, ranging from classical supervised pipelines to modern self-supervised foundation models, handle the unique constraints of tiny and even featureless targets in spaceborne video. We categorize our benchmarked methods into three primary methodological trajectories:

\textbf{Region Proposal and Detection-Based Tracking}: We select representative models from the early deep learning era that established the foundation for supervised tracking. This includes two-stage baselines like Faster R-CNN and paradigms like Tracktor, which leverage region proposal networks (RPN) to identify targets and subsequently associate them across frames. These models serve as a classical reference for understanding the performance of explicit, frame-by-frame supervised detection in satellite imagery.

\textbf{Spatiotemporal Fusion Models}: To address the limitations of independent frame processing, we select architectures that explicitly model the time dimension. This line includes spatiotemporal Transformers (e.g., HRLT, TATrans) and joint detection and tracking (JDT) frameworks that couple appearance and motion features during training. We also include unified fusion frameworks like STAR, which integrates deep temporal features and physical motion priors (e.g., Kalman filters) to maintain identity consistency during long-term occlusions in non-stationary backgrounds.

\textbf{Large Geospatial and Vision Foundation Models}: Reflecting the most recent paradigm shift, we evaluate large-scale foundation models such as SAM 2 and DINO v3. These models are selected for their robust zero-shot generalization and ability to leverage high-quality feature representations learned from vast amounts of unlabeled data. By evaluating prompt-driven video segmentation and tracking, we explore the potential of transfer learning to overcome the extreme scarcity of labeled spaceborne data.

\subsection{Performance analysis w.r.t. object attributes}
The model performance w.r.t to different object attributes on the dataset SV248S is shown in Tab.~\ref{tab:sot_attributes_sv248s}. We can conclude the following findings.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LoRAT-g378 dominates among pretrained trackers.}
    It ranks first on most attributes, with an especially commanding margin
    over other pretrained baselines on \emph{Natural Disturbance} (ND) and
    \emph{Slow Motion} (SM).
    \item \textbf{SAM~3 excels under occlusion.} On both
    \emph{Continuous Occlusion} (CO) and \emph{Long-Term Occlusion} (LTO),
    SAM~3 attains the top score across all three metrics. This suggests
    that the memory-bank mechanism of the SAM family confers a zero-shot
    advantage in recovering targets after prolonged occlusion -- a regime
    in which discriminatively trained trackers typically drift.
    \item \textbf{Counterexample on Background Cluster (BCL).}
    The pretrained LoRAT-g378 and OSTrack-384 outperform the zero-shot
    SAM~3 here, indicating that under the dense, visually homogeneous
    backgrounds characteristic of satellite video, supervised fine-tuning
    on remote-sensing data can still matter more than zero-shot
    generalization.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}



\section{Conclusion}
We introduced Space-tracker, the first unified and curated "million-scale" benchmark dataset designed for the detection and tracking of small targets in spaceborne videos. By merging XX disparate datasets into a consistent COCO-style framework, we provide a singular diagnostic platform to address the unique geometric and radiometric constraints of the aerospace domain. Our primary contribution lies in the unification of 12 fine-grained attributes, such as sub-pixel target size and non-stationary backgrounds, enabling a rigorous assessment of tracker failure modes across VNIR and thermal spectra. Extensive benchmarking of seven state-of-the-art models reveals that while modern architectures excel at motion modeling, sub-pixel accuracy and long-term occlusion remain critical technical bottlenecks. Space-tracker establishes a necessary performance baseline for advancing real-time monitoring applications in high-stakes domains such as disaster management and environmental conservation. Moving forward, we commit to the long-term maintenance of this benchmark through an open evaluation server to ensure its continued utility as an evolving standard for the research community.
