\hypertarget{dataset-preprocessing}{%
\subsection{Dataset preprocessing}\label{dataset-preprocessing}}

The preprocessing pipeline consists of two main steps: splitting each dataset into training, validation, and test subsets, and applying data augmentation during training to improve model generalisation.

\subsubsection*{Dataset Split}

Each dataset is partitioned into three non-overlapping subsets: train, validation, and test. The splitting strategy is adapted to the structure and official annotations available for each dataset.

\textbf{OOTB.} The OOTB dataset contains 110 videos across four object categories: car (45), plane (25), ship (30), and train (10). Since no official split is provided, a stratified random split with ratio \textbf{80\,/\,10\,/\,10} is applied, using \texttt{seed=42} to ensure reproducibility. Stratification is performed by category to preserve class balance across all three subsets. The resulting split is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:ootb-split}.

\textbf{BIRDSAI.} The BIRDSAI dataset provides an official train/test partition (\texttt{TrainReal} / \texttt{TestReal}). The training set is taken directly from \texttt{TrainReal}. A validation subset is derived from \texttt{TestReal} using a stratified split by source video (\textbf{30\,/\,70}, \texttt{seed=42}), yielding a dedicated validation set while preserving the remaining test sequences. Two tracking configurations are used:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{tracking\_split="perfect"}: only non-occluded frames are retained. The split yields 383 train sequences (70,261 frames), 25 val sequences (5,575 frames), and 138 test sequences (28,170 frames) across 34 source videos.
  \item \texttt{tracking\_split="full"}: occluded frames are included in the annotations but skipped during data loading. This results in 268 train sequences (82,004 valid frames), 23 val sequences (6,109 frames), and 73 test sequences (64,171 frames) across 48 source videos.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{BIRDSAI\_MOT.} The BIRDSAI\_MOT variant uses raw MOT-format CSV annotations with two object categories: \texttt{animal} (class 0) and \texttt{human} (class 1). The \texttt{TrainReal} partition is used for training, while \texttt{TestReal} is divided into val (30\%) and test (70\%) by video (\texttt{seed=42}). This produces 32 train videos (21,209 frames, 87,199 boxes), 5 val videos (3,258 frames, 6,856 boxes), and 11 test videos (12,236 frames, 71,905 boxes), for a total of 165,960 annotated bounding boxes.

\subsubsection*{Data Augmentation}

All image and bounding-box transforms are implemented using the \texttt{albumentations} library~\cite{albumentations}, with bounding boxes represented in Pascal VOC format (\texttt{xyxy} absolute pixel coordinates). A minimum visibility threshold of 0.3 is applied: any bounding box that retains less than 30\% of its original area after a spatial transform is automatically discarded from the annotation.

Two separate transform pipelines are defined:

\textbf{Training transform.} During training, each frame is resized to $640 \times 640$ pixels and then subjected to a random horizontal flip with probability $p = 0.5$. The horizontal flip simulates the natural left--right symmetry of aerial and satellite observations, increasing the diversity of object orientations seen by the model without introducing unrealistic artefacts.

\textbf{Evaluation transform.} During validation and testing, only a deterministic resize to $640 \times 640$ pixels is applied, with no stochastic operations, ensuring reproducible evaluation metrics across runs.

The choice of a minimal augmentation strategy is intentional: the datasets contain infrared and thermal imagery with specific spectral properties, and aggressive photometric augmentations (e.g.\ colour jitter or channel dropout) could distort the radiometric information that detectors rely on for small target discrimination.

\hypertarget{algorithms-assessment}{%
\section{Algorithms assessment}\label{algorithms-assessment}}

In the Algorithms Theoretical Basis Description D2, we introduced the advanced algorithms we intended to investigate and compare, including SAM2, Faster R-CNN, YOLO, and DINOv3. 
In the Test Specification, we also introduced the SOT and MOT tasks respectively. 
The reason for discussing them as two separate tasks is that the datasets mentioned in the Reference Data Selection section vary in applicability — some are only suitable for the SOT task, while others are applicable to both SOT and MOT tasks. 
This is related to the type of annotations provided by each dataset.

\hypertarget{Single Object Tracking (SOT)}{%
\subsection{Single Object Tracking (SOT)}\label{SOT}}

From the very definition of the SOT task, it is clear that not all algorithms are suited for it. 
SAM2 is applicable, as it requires prompts in the form of a bounding box, segmentation mask, or points from the first frame to identify and confirm the object to be detected and tracked throughout the video. 
In contrast, traditional object detection algorithms such as Faster R-CNN and YOLO are primarily focused on detecting object categories and bounding boxes in individual frames. 
Critically, they are not designed to accept a first-frame bounding box as input, which means they lack the ability to associate a specific target instance across frames — a fundamental requirement of SOT. 
They are therefore clearly unsuitable for the SOT task and have been excluded from our SOT evaluation accordingly.

\hypertarget{Multi-Object Tracking (MOT)}{%
\subsection{Multi-Object Tracking (MOT)}\label{MOT}}

As can be inferred from the definition of the MOT task, the algorithms previously introduced in the Algorithms Theoretical Basis Description — namely Faster R-CNN and YOLO (each coupled with ByteTracker), as well as SAM2 — are all applicable to this task. 
Unlike SOT, MOT does not require a first-frame prompt to initialise a specific target; instead, it relies on detecting and associating all objects of interest across frames. 
Faster R-CNN and YOLO, when coupled with ByteTracker, are well-suited for this paradigm, as they provide per-frame detections that ByteTracker then links into consistent trajectories. 
SAM2 is likewise applicable, leveraging its segmentation and tracking capabilities in a multi-object setting.
